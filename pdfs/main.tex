\begin{abstract}
The ongoing integration of European electricity markets toward a single wholesale price zone, coupled with the proliferation of cross-border interconnections, is creating increasingly large multi-scale power systems that must be operated and planned as a whole. At the same time, the growing frequency of black-swan events---extreme heat waves, prolonged wind droughts, cascading grid failures---demands the ability to solve unit-commitment and dispatch problems very rapidly under unprecedented stress conditions. Power-system flexibility studies therefore increasingly demand \emph{scenario exploration} rather than single-shot optimisation: analysts must evaluate large ensembles of ``what-if'' cases that combine multi-scale resources (storage, demand response, inter-area exchanges), network limits, evolving topology, and correlated uncertainty. Mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) provides rigorous and auditable answers, but repeated solves quickly become prohibitive as scenario volume and complexity grow. We propose a hybrid \textbf{MILP--GNN--EBM} methodology designed for near-real-time exploration. A multi-layer MILP oracle is first solved on a diversity-maximising scenario set to generate optimal labels. Each instance is then represented as a heterogeneous temporal graph and processed by a topology-consistent graph neural network (GNN) that produces compact scenario embeddings. Conditioned on these embeddings, an energy-based model (EBM) learns an energy landscape over binary activation/commitment decisions and enables fast sampling of multiple high-quality candidates via Langevin dynamics. A lightweight feasibility decoder repairs residual violations, and a conditional LP worker recovers the continuous dispatch to produce complete, economically meaningful operating plans. We evaluate speed, feasibility, and optimality across scenario families of increasing difficulty, testing whether the MILP--GNN--EBM stack provides larger benefits as scenarios become more complex.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Power System; Multi-Scale Modelling; MILP; Graph Neural Networks; Energy-Based Models; Scenario Exploration.

\section{Introduction}

Power systems are undergoing a profound transformation, driven by the large-scale integration of variable renewable energy (VRE), rapid electrification of end uses, and the growing penetration of distributed energy resources (DER) such as demand response (DR) and storage. This transition pushes system operators and planners to mobilize flexibility across \emph{multiple spatial layers} (from behind-the-meter assets to regional and national transmission systems) and \emph{multiple temporal scales} (from intra-hour balancing to seasonal adequacy), while guaranteeing feasibility under network, market, and security constraints. Two converging trends amplify this challenge. On the structural side, the progressive integration of European electricity markets---toward a single wholesale price zone with dense cross-border interconnections---is producing power systems of unprecedented spatial scale that must be co-optimised across hundreds of zones and multiple voltage levels. On the operational side, the increasing frequency of black-swan events (extreme heat waves, prolonged Dunkelflaute episodes, cascading equipment failures) creates urgent demand for near-real-time solving of large-scale unit-commitment and economic-dispatch problems under stress conditions that lie far outside historical experience.

A central methodological challenge is that the questions stakeholders increasingly ask are not single-run questions (``what is the optimal schedule today?''), but \emph{interactive what-if questions} that require exploring thousands of scenarios: extreme weather and correlated forecast errors, congestion and topology changes, new flexibility portfolios (storage and DR), and coupled infrastructures (e.g., gas--power--carbon interactions). In these settings, the practical bottleneck is not only solving one high-fidelity instance, but enabling \emph{near-real-time scenario exploration} where users can turn a ``complexity knob'' (more spatial detail, longer horizons, tighter constraints, more uncertainty) and still obtain fast, credible answers.

Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) provides a rigorous and auditable representation of these decisions, and remains the reference tool for high-fidelity scheduling and planning \parencite{conejo_investment_2016}. However, repeated MILP solves become rapidly prohibitive as scenario count and model complexity increase, due to non-convexities (e.g., unit commitment logic) and strong inter-temporal and network couplings. Conversely, learning-based surrogates---and in particular Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)---can exploit grid topology to deliver fast predictions, but purely predictive approaches can violate hard constraints, drift out of distribution, and lose economic meaning.

This paper proposes a hybrid \textbf{MILP--GNN--EBM methodology for multi-scale flexibility exploration} that turns high-fidelity optimization into an exploration engine. Concretely, we propose an end-to-end pipeline that couples synthetic scenario generation, a multi-layer MILP oracle, topology-consistent graph representations, and learning-based inference with feasibility-preserving post-processing.

Rather than replacing optimization, the proposed surrogate stack aims to preserve MILP-level structure while enabling \emph{near-real-time} scenario sweeps: the MILP produces ground truth, the GNN compresses scenarios into informative representations, the EBM provides controlled exploration over binaries, and the decoder+LP stages restore feasibility and compute the final dispatch. The objective is not to predict a single optimal solution, but to enable fast and controlled exploration of high-quality feasible solutions as scenario complexity increases.


\subsection{Literature Review}

MILP remains the workhorse for operational scheduling (e.g., unit commitment and multi-period dispatch) and long-term planning under uncertainty, due to its ability to encode discrete decisions, operational constraints, and economic objectives in a transparent manner \parencite{conejo_investment_2016}. In expansion planning, stochastic and multi-stage MILP formulations capture uncertainty in costs and renewable availability, but typically require decomposition, scenario reduction, or aggregation to remain tractable \parencite{micheli_two-stage_2023}. Beyond single-level optimization, multi-level and equilibrium-aware formulations have been developed to represent strategic interactions and sequential market clearings: early MPEC-based approaches model strategic bidding under network constraints \parencite{hobbs_strategic_2000}, while three-level MILP formulations integrate transmission expansion, generation investment, and market equilibrium at the operational layer \parencite{pozo_three-level_2013}. More recently, scalable approximations for mixed-integer multi-level problems with sequential followers have been proposed, together with dedicated Benders-type decomposition strategies to address computational scaling in coupled electricity--gas--carbon market settings \parencite{xia_scalable_2025}.

Building on these MILP foundations, when flexibility is distributed across buildings, districts, and regions, MILP models can explicitly represent coordination across spatial layers, but the number of integer variables and coupling constraints grows rapidly with resolution and horizon length. Holarchic or hierarchical formulations mitigate this growth through structured aggregation and decomposition, enabling urban- or district-scale optimization while preserving key network interactions \parencite{marquant_holarchic_2017}. Robust hierarchical MILP designs further address uncertainty (e.g., demand ranges) via min--max constructs, at the cost of additional layers and higher computational burden \parencite{yokoyama_hierarchical_2021}. Overall, the literature shows a consistent tension: high-fidelity multi-layer MILPs are auditable and economically meaningful, but they remain expensive for large scenario sweeps and interactive exploration.

To alleviate this computational bottleneck, recent work has explored GNNs as topology-aware surrogates or accelerators. Early work showed that graph neural architectures can emulate load-flow/physics solvers and generalize across topologies \parencite{donon_graph_2019}, and can approximate OPF solutions via imitation learning on AC settings \parencite{owerko_optimal_2019}. For AC optimal power flow (AC-OPF), topology-informed GNNs have been designed with feasibility regularization and adaptation mechanisms for topology contingencies \parencite{liu_topology-aware_2023}. Complementary approaches use GNNs to provide warm-starts or initial estimates, reducing the iteration count of interior-point solvers and thereby accelerating high-fidelity OPF computations \parencite{deihim_initial_2024}. Other studies benchmark GNN architectures (e.g., attention-based) for efficient AC power flow or OPF prediction in larger grids \parencite{talebi_graph_2025}. Beyond OPF, GNN proxies have been trained from large collections of MILP solves to support hours-ahead risk assessment under evolving topology, with reported gains in speed and situational awareness \parencite{zhang_graph_2024}. In restoration and UC-like settings, learning-based policies have also been proposed to accelerate MILP-based decision making, e.g., for load restoration \parencite{ahmed_advancing_2024}, by predicting an initial commitment refined via large neighborhood search \parencite{yang_solving_nodate}, or by directly learning branching/diving policies in modern MIP solvers \parencite{qin_solve_2023}. Related hybrid strategies combine GNN predictions with LP relaxations to fix stable variables and accelerate UC while controlling suboptimality \parencite{yang_stable_2025}, and confidence-aware architectures seed commitment and active-constraint predictions to speed up large industrial RAC formulations \parencite{park_confidence-aware_2023}. At the planning level, GNN surrogates have been proposed to approximate the outputs of MILP energy system models, enabling faster what-if analyses \parencite{pjetri_graph_nodate}.

A persistent challenge in learning-to-optimize is enforcing hard constraints and providing controlled exploration of complex discrete spaces. Two complementary strands are particularly relevant: (i) learning to make discrete decisions inside MILP solvers, and (ii) learning generative/energy-based models for exploration.

On the solver side, MILPs admit a natural bipartite constraint--variable graph representation that can be exploited by GNN policies for branching decisions \parencite{gasse_exact_2019}. However, fundamental representational limits also exist: there are MILP families that all GNNs will treat equally unless additional structure or features are injected \parencite{chen_representing_2023}, motivating hybrid designs where learning is guided by optimization structure.

On the generative side, energy-based formulations offer a principled bridge: combinatorial objectives can be written as energies, and neural networks can be trained to approximate these energies and sample high-quality solutions. In particular, annealed training has been proposed to cast graph combinatorial optimization problems as unbiased EBMs with carefully selected penalties, yielding more stable unsupervised training and improved solution quality \parencite{sun_annealed_2022}. Progress in discrete sampling benchmarks further highlights the importance of robust, scalable samplers for discrete/structured distributions \parencite{goshvadi_discs_2023}. EBMs have also been explored for non-convex, cardinality-constrained portfolio optimization, illustrating GPU-accelerated sampling as an alternative to single-solution pipelines \parencite{mancilla_non-convex_2026}.

In parallel, differentiable satisfiability and projection layers have been developed to enforce general linear constraints (including those arising in unit commitment), offering end-to-end differentiable mechanisms to project neural outputs back into feasible sets \parencite{zeng_glinsat_2024}. When scenario exploration involves composite and time-varying action spaces (e.g., heterogeneous flexibility levers becoming available/unavailable), structured cooperative RL provides additional design patterns for handling such changing decision sets \parencite{li_structured_2021}. More broadly, generator-enhanced optimization frameworks leverage generative models (classical or quantum-inspired) to improve search-space exploration for hard optimization problems \parencite{alcazar_enhancing_2024}.

\subsection{Research Gap and Research Statement}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{(\arabic*)}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Scenario exploration at scale is still solver-bound.} Multi-layer and multi-level MILP formulations remain too costly to support near-real-time exploration when the number of scenarios grows (stress cases, coupled markets, topology changes), despite decomposition and aggregation strategies \parencite{micheli_two-stage_2023,pozo_three-level_2013,xia_scalable_2025,marquant_holarchic_2017}.
  \item \emph{Learning surrogates rarely deliver end-to-end feasibility across layers.} GNN surrogates can be accurate and topology-aware for specific tasks (OPF, risk assessment, restoration), yet guaranteeing feasibility across heterogeneous layers (assets--zones--regions) and across long horizons remains difficult without an explicit feasibility mechanism \parencite{liu_topology-aware_2023,zhang_graph_2024,ahmed_advancing_2024}.
  \item \emph{Limited support for controlled exploration under increasing scenario complexity.} Existing surrogates are often trained for prediction; fewer approaches explicitly address exploration of large discrete decision spaces (commitment logic, discrete DR activation) in a way that remains robust as scenario complexity increases \parencite{yang_solving_nodate,sun_annealed_2022,goshvadi_discs_2023}.
  \item \emph{Weak coupling between economic signals and learned inference.} Multi-level/equilibrium MILP models yield meaningful price signals and strategic interactions, but most learning pipelines do not exploit this structure to steer inference or sampling toward economically consistent solutions \parencite{hobbs_strategic_2000,pozo_three-level_2013,xia_scalable_2025}.
  \item \emph{From point predictions to interactive, near-real-time what-if analysis.} Warm-starting and surrogate prediction accelerate single runs, but the literature provides limited methodology for interactive, user-driven scenario sweeps where the ``complexity knob'' (spatial detail, coupling, uncertainty, topology changes) is increased while preserving speed, feasibility, and interpretability \parencite{deihim_initial_2024,pjetri_graph_nodate,talebi_graph_2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{}
The central research question is therefore:
\emph{Can a hybrid MILP--GNN--EBM methodology enable near-real-time exploration of multi-scale flexibility scenarios---and remain effective as scenarios become increasingly complex (heterogeneous layers, discrete decisions, uncertainty, and topology change)?}

\subsection{Contributions}
Our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{A flexibility scenario generator with diversity maximisation and complexity indexing.} We sample a wide range of techno-economic parameters to maximise scenario diversity and compute a scenario-specific complexity indicator that ranks instances by expected difficulty.
  \item \textbf{A multi-layer MILP oracle for coordinated flexibility.} We formulate and solve a high-fidelity MILP that coordinates multi-scale flexibility resources under network and inter-temporal constraints, producing auditable primal schedules (and optional dual signals).
  \item \textbf{A projection of scenario instances to hierarchical multi-layer temporal graphs.} Each scenario is encoded as a heterogeneous temporal graph that mirrors the nested physical/aggregation structure (assets $\to$ zones $\to$ regions $\to$ system) while preserving time-coupled constraints.
  \item \textbf{A topology-consistent GNN encoder for scenario embeddings.} We learn a GNN encoder that captures scenario structure and maps each instance into an embedding space that preserves the hierarchical, multi-layer, and temporal organisation of the system.
  \item \textbf{A graph EBM sampler with feasibility-preserving decoding.} Conditioned on scenario embeddings, an EBM learns an energy landscape over binary decisions and enables fast sampling; a lightweight decoder repairs residual violations, and a conditional LP worker recovers continuous dispatch.
  \item \textbf{An evaluation protocol for large-scale scenario exploration.} We quantify speed, feasibility, and optimality across scenario families of increasing complexity, testing how performance evolves as the exploration ``complexity knob'' is increased.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Paper Organization}
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~2 introduces the overall pipeline and details the scenario generation process and the multi-layer MILP oracle used to produce optimal labels for training. Section~3 presents the hierarchical heterogeneous temporal graph representation and the topology-consistent encoder that maps each scenario to compact embeddings. Section~4 describes the conditional graph EBM and the Langevin-based sampling strategy for discrete decision candidates. Section~5 is dedicated to the post-processing stages that restore feasibility and recover continuous dispatch through a feasibility decoder and an LP worker. Section~6 presents the experimental setup and results, structured around three criticality families (low, medium, high) with robust distributional metrics, a systematic discussion of when the MILP remains superior and where the pipeline fails, and an energy-system perspective that frames the results in terms of near-real-time flexibility assessment, climate stress testing, and flexibility procurement. Finally, Section~7 concludes and outlines the current limitations and directions for future work, including security constraints, uncertainty extensions, and deployment-oriented robustness mechanisms.


\section{Methodology Overview and Training Data Generation}
Figure~\ref{fig:milp-gnn-ebm-pipeline} presents the proposed MILP--GNN--EBM pipeline as an end-to-end exploration engine that transforms high-fidelity optimization into fast and controllable large-scale scenario sweeps. Starting from a broad pool of synthetic what-if cases, the workflow proceeds as follows: (i) we generate a diversity-maximizing set of scenarios and compute for each of them a scenario-level complexity index capturing combinatorial and operational stress; (ii) a multi-layer MILP oracle is solved to produce optimal solutions; (iii) each instance is projected onto a hierarchical, heterogeneous temporal graph and encoded via a topology-consistent GNN into a compact latent representation; (iv) a conditional graph EBM is trained to efficiently explore the space of high-quality discrete decisions; (v) candidate solutions are then repaired and completed through a feasibility-aware decoder and a conditional LP worker to recover continuous dispatch variables; finally, (vi) the resulting solutions are benchmarked with scenario families of increasing complexity against the pure MILP oracle in terms of runtime, feasibility rate, and cost optimality gap.

\subsection{Scenario Generator}
\label{sec:scenario_generator}

The first stage of our pipeline is a \emph{synthetic scenario generator} that produces a diverse set of power-system flexibility instances to train and evaluate the downstream learning modules. Each scenario encodes a complete specification of the system: network topology, asset portfolio, economic policies, technical constraints, and exogenous drivers (weather, demand, hydro inflows). The generator is designed to (i)~span a wide range of physical stress levels and combinatorial hardness, (ii)~remain computationally tractable for MILP labelling, and (iii)~avoid redundant near-duplicate instances that waste compute budget.

\subsubsection{Scenario Parameter Space}
\label{sec:scenario_space}

A scenario $s$ is fully characterized by a configuration tuple
\begin{equation}
s = \bigl(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{A}, \pi, \tau, c, \xi\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{G}$ is the network graph, $\mathcal{A}$ is the asset specification, $\pi$ denotes economic policy parameters, $\tau$ collects technical scalers, $c$ represents operation costs, and $\xi$ captures exogenous drivers.

\paragraph{Network structure $\mathcal{G}$.}
The spatial hierarchy follows a \emph{assets $\rightarrow$ zones $\rightarrow$ regions $\rightarrow$ nation} structure. For each scenario we sample:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item number of regions $|\mathcal{R}|$,
  \item zones per region $|\mathcal{Z}_r|$ (sampled independently per region),
  \item assets are sampled either at the zone level ($|\mathcal{A}_z$|) or at the region level ($|\mathcal{A}_r$|), depending on the technology (e.g., nuclear units are regional, while solar units are zonal),
  \item intertie density $\rho$ controlling inter-regional transmission,
  \item number of neighbouring nations $|\mathcal{N}|$ for cross-border exchanges.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Asset portfolio $\mathcal{A}$.}
Asset counts are sampled at zone or regional level:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item thermal, run-of-river hydro, solar and wind units per zone,
  \item battery storage and demand-response resources per zone,
  \item nuclear plants, reservoir hydro and pumped-hydro storage per region
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Economic policy $\pi$.}
Policy levers include CO$_2$ price (EUR/t), price cap (EUR/MWh), cross-border policy (free, limited, or blocked), and import/export caps.

\paragraph{Technical scalers $\tau$.}
Key flexibility drivers include thermal ramp rates, minimum generation ratios, battery energy-to-power ratio, round-trip efficiency, final state-of-charge tolerances, and demand-response parameters (max shed share, duration, number of activation events, ramp limits).

\paragraph{Exogenous drivers $\xi$.}
Weather and load conditions are captured by:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item a dominant weather profile (e.g., sunny summer, stormy winter, cloudy spring),
  \item per-region weather cells with profile and spread intensity,
  \item demand profile (weekday peak, weekend flat, etc.) and demand scale factor,
  \item hydro inflow factor.
\end{itemize}
To preserve spatial heterogeneity, each region draws its own weather profile; summary statistics (histogram, entropy, min/mean/max spread) are retained for diversity computation.

\subsubsection{Budget Guard}
\label{sec:budget_guard}

Not all randomly sampled scenarios are tractable within a fixed compute budget. Before acceptance, each candidate $s$ passes through a \emph{budget guard} that estimates MILP size and solve time:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{var}}(s) = f_{\mathrm{var}}(\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{A}, |\mathcal{T}|),
\end{equation}
where $|\mathcal{T}|$ is the number of time steps. A polynomial time estimator then predicts solve time:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{t}(s) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \widehat{n}_{\mathrm{var}} + \beta_2 \widehat{n}_{\mathrm{cont}} + \beta_3 \widehat{n}_{\mathrm{bin}},
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{var}}$ is the estimated total number of decision variables,
$\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{cont}}$ the estimated number of continuous variables,
and $\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{bin}}$ the estimated number of binary variables
(unit commitment, nuclear commitment, and demand-response activation flags). Scenarios exceeding thresholds on variables, constraints, or predicted solve time are rejected.

\subsubsection{Diversity Maximization}
\label{sec:diversity}

Uniform random sampling tends to cluster in high-dimensional parameter space and over-represents ``easy'' instances. We employ two complementary strategies to maximize useful diversity.

\paragraph{Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS).}
For continuous parameters that drive stress and difficulty (demand scale, inflow factor, CO$_2$ price, intertie density, ramp percentages, storage E/P ratio, import caps), we replace uniform sampling with Latin Hypercube Sampling. LHS partitions each marginal into $N$ equiprobable intervals and ensures exactly one sample per interval, yielding more uniform coverage in high dimensions. Compared to uniform random sampling, LHS reduces clustering and ensures better coverage of extreme parameter combinations.

\paragraph{Greedy $k$-center selection.}
Rather than accepting candidates online, we generate a large candidate pool (e.g., $20 \times$ target size) passing the budget guard, then select the final set via a greedy farthest-point algorithm:
\begin{equation}
s_{t+1} = \arg\max_{s \in \mathcal{P}} \min_{s' \in \mathcal{S}_t} d\bigl(v(s), v(s')\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{P}$ is the candidate pool, $\mathcal{S}_t$ is the selected set, and $v(s) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a normalized \emph{diversity vector} encoding topology, asset mix, technical scalers, and exogenous drivers. This ensures a minimum pairwise separation $d_{\min}$ and avoids near-duplicates. Intuitively, this procedure iteratively selects scenarios that are maximally dissimilar from those already selected, thereby spreading the final dataset over the diversity space and avoiding redundant instances.


\paragraph{}
LHS is applied at the parameter-sampling stage to ensure marginal coverage of key continuous drivers, while greedy $k$-center selection operates at the scenario level to enforce global diversity across structural, technical, and exogenous dimensions.

\paragraph{Stratification.}
To prevent over-representation of easy scenarios, we partition candidates into bins along stress and hardness axes (e.g., demand-scale quintiles, topology-size quartiles, weather-profile categories) and allocate quotas per bin before $k$-center selection. Stratification ensures that rare but operationally critical scenarios are sufficiently represented in the training set, rather than being drowned out by a majority of mild instances.


\subsubsection{Scenario Criticality Indicator}
\label{sec:criticality}

To characterize scenario difficulty \emph{ex ante} (before solving), we define a composite criticality indicator decomposed into physical stress and combinatorial hardness:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Crit}(s) = \alpha \, \mathrm{Stress}(s) + (1-\alpha) \, \mathrm{Hard}(s), \quad \alpha \in [0,1].
\end{equation}

All components of $\mathrm{Crit}(s)$ are computable ex ante, without solving the MILP.

\paragraph{Physical Stress Index.}
The stress component captures techno-operational tension through 12 normalized metrics grouped into four categories:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item \textbf{Variability:} VRE penetration ratio, residual-demand volatility, peak-to-valley ratio, short-term variability index.
  \item \textbf{Load level:} demand scale factor, peak-to-firm capacity ratio.
  \item \textbf{Flexibility margins (inverse):} storage power adequacy, storage energy adequacy, thermal flexibility ratio, DR headroom.
  \item \textbf{Spatial tension:} import/export reliance, congestion exposure proxy.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Combinatorial Hardness Index.}
The hardness component captures structural MILP difficulty through 10 metrics:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep, label=(\alph*)]
  \item \textbf{Problem size:} number of zones $|\mathcal{Z}|$, horizon length $|\mathcal{T}|$, estimated binary count.
  \item \textbf{Non-convexity:} thermal unit density, minimum-generation tightness, startup-cost intensity.
  \item \textbf{Temporal coupling:} ramping tightness, SOC tightness proxy.
  \item \textbf{Spatial coupling:} interconnection density, network heterogeneity.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{}
These metrics were selected to jointly capture (i) known drivers of operational stress in power systems and (ii) structural sources of MILP hardness identified in the unit-commitment literature \parencite{buchheim_polytope_2023, huang_cutting_2021, morales_espana_tight_2013}. For completeness and reproducibility, Appendix~\ref{app:criticality} reports the definitions of the 22 metrics used to build the ex ante criticality index.


\paragraph{}
Each metric is normalized to $[0,1]$ using generator-bound scaling and combined as weighted sums:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Stress}(s) = \sum_{i=1}^{12} w_i^{(S)} \tilde{x}_i^{(S)}(s), \quad
\mathrm{Hard}(s) = \sum_{j=1}^{10} w_j^{(H)} \tilde{x}_j^{(H)}(s).
\end{equation}
The criticality index enables stratified sampling and post-hoc analysis of solver performance versus scenario difficulty.

\subsection{Multi-Layer MILP Oracle}
\label{sec:milp_oracle}

The second stage of the pipeline solves, for each generated scenario, a high-fidelity mixed-integer linear program (MILP) that coordinates flexibility resources across the spatial hierarchy defined in Section~\ref{sec:scenario_generator}. The model is a multi-period unit-commitment and economic dispatch formulation that jointly optimizes discrete commitment and activation decisions together with continuous power and storage schedules, subject to network, inter-temporal, and operational constraints. Its solutions provide the ground-truth labels~$(u^*, x^*)$ used to train all downstream learning modules.

Rather than enumerating all modelling variants, we report the oracle’s main constraint families,
with emphasis on discrete decisions and inter-temporal couplings that drive MILP hardness.



\subsubsection{Sets and Indexing}
\label{sec:milp_sets}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $\mathcal{Z}$: set of zones (nested within regions according to the spatial hierarchy of Section~\ref{sec:scenario_space}).
  \item $\mathcal{T} = \{0, 1, \dots, T{-}1\}$: set of time steps of uniform duration $\Delta t$ (hours).
  \item $\mathcal{L}$: set of transmission lines connecting zones (intra-regional and inter-regional).
  \item $\mathcal{K} = \{0, 1, \dots, K{-}1\}$: set of demand-response cost tiers (blocks).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Decision Variables}
\label{sec:milp_variables}

The model distinguishes two variable families whose separation is central to the downstream learning pipeline.

\paragraph{Binary (commitment and activation) variables $u$.}
These encode discrete on/off or mode decisions that the EBM sampler will learn to predict:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} \in \{0,1\}$: thermal commitment status in zone~$z$ at time~$t$.
  \item $v^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} \in \{0,1\}$: thermal startup indicator ($v^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = 1$ if the unit starts up at~$t$).
  \item $\delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t} \in \{0,1\}$: demand-response activation flag.
  \item $\sigma^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t} \in \{0,1\}$: battery charge/discharge mode ($1$=charge, $0$=discharge). 
  \item $\sigma^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t} \in \{0,1\}$: pumped-hydro charge/discharge mode.
  \item $\sigma^{\mathrm{imp}}_{t} \in \{0,1\}$: cross-border import/export mode ($1$=import).
\end{itemize}

The mode binaries enforce mutual exclusivity while minimizing the number of discrete variables.

\paragraph{Continuous (dispatch and state) variables $x$.}
These are recovered by the LP worker once binaries are fixed:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}, p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: thermal and nuclear power output.
  \item $p^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t}, p^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: dispatched solar and wind power.
  \item $s^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t}, s^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: curtailed (spilled) solar and wind power.
  \item $d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: total demand-response shed; $d^{\mathrm{dr},k}_{z,t} \geq 0$: shed per tier~$k$.
  \item $e^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: demand-response rebound energy backlog.
  \item $q^{\mathrm{uns}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: unserved energy (load shedding of last resort).
  \item $b^{+}_{z,t}, b^{-}_{z,t} \geq 0$: battery charge and discharge power.
  \item $e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: battery state of charge (SOC).
  \item $h^{\mathrm{rel}}_{z,t}, h^{\mathrm{spl}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: reservoir hydro release and spillage.
  \item $e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: reservoir hydro level.
  \item $g^{+}_{z,t}, g^{-}_{z,t} \geq 0$: pumped-hydro charge and discharge power.
  \item $e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: pumped-hydro storage level.
  \item $f_{\ell,t} \in \mathbb{R}$: power flow on line~$\ell$ (bidirectional).
  \item $m^{+}_{t}, m^{-}_{t} \geq 0$: cross-border import and export power.
  \item $q^{\mathrm{ovg}}_{z,t} \geq 0$: over-generation spillage.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Objective Function}
\label{sec:milp_objective}

The objective minimizes total system operating cost over the horizon:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:objective}
\min_{u,x} \; C^{\mathrm{gen}} + C^{\mathrm{su}} + C^{\mathrm{dr}} + C^{\mathrm{uns}} + C^{\mathrm{spl}} + C^{\mathrm{sto}} + C^{\mathrm{imp}} + C^{\mathrm{ovg}},
\end{equation}
where each component aggregates over all zones and time steps:
\begin{align}
C^{\mathrm{gen}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} \bigl( c^{\mathrm{th}}_z \, p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} + c^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z \, p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t} \bigr), \label{eq:cost_gen} \\
C^{\mathrm{su}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} c^{\mathrm{su}}_z \, v^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}, \label{eq:cost_startup} \\
C^{\mathrm{dr}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} c^{\mathrm{dr}}_k \, d^{\mathrm{dr},k}_{z,t}, \label{eq:cost_dr} \\
C^{\mathrm{uns}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} c^{\mathrm{voll}} \, q^{\mathrm{uns}}_{z,t}, \label{eq:cost_uns} \\
C^{\mathrm{spl}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} \bigl( c^{\mathrm{res}} (s^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t} + s^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t}) + c^{\mathrm{hsp}} \, h^{\mathrm{spl}}_{z,t} \bigr), \label{eq:cost_spill} \\
C^{\mathrm{sto}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} \bigl( c^{\mathrm{cyc,bat}}_z (b^{+}_{z,t} + b^{-}_{z,t}) + c^{\mathrm{cyc,phs}}_z (g^{+}_{z,t} + g^{-}_{z,t}) \bigr), \label{eq:cost_storage} \\
C^{\mathrm{imp}} &= \textstyle\sum_{t} \bigl( c^{\mathrm{imp}} \, m^{+}_{t} + c^{\mathrm{exp}} \, m^{-}_{t} \bigr), \label{eq:cost_trade} \\
C^{\mathrm{ovg}} &= \textstyle\sum_{z,t} c^{\mathrm{ovg}} \, q^{\mathrm{ovg}}_{z,t}. \label{eq:cost_overgen}
\end{align}

The thermal fuel cost~$c^{\mathrm{th}}_z$ includes a CO$_2$ component ($c^{\mathrm{th}}_z = c^{\mathrm{fuel}} + c^{\mathrm{marg}} + \pi_{\mathrm{CO}_2} \cdot \varepsilon$), and the demand-response tier costs~$c^{\mathrm{dr}}_k$ increase exponentially across blocks ($c^{\mathrm{dr}}_k = c^{\mathrm{dr}}_0 \cdot 1.5^{k}$) to model increasing activation reluctance. The value of lost load $c^{\mathrm{voll}}$ acts as a large penalty ensuring load shedding is a last resort.

\subsubsection{Constraint Families}
\label{sec:milp_constraints}

The model enforces nine families of constraints whose full mathematical formulation is given in Appendix~\ref{app:milp_constraints}. We summarize them here to convey the sources of MILP hardness.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{(\roman*)}, noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Thermal unit commitment.}
    Output is bounded between minimum stable generation and rated capacity, scaled by the commitment binary~$u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}$. Symmetric ramp-up/down limits couple consecutive periods, and a startup indicator $v^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}$ detects $0{\to}1$ commitment transitions to incur startup costs.

  \item \textbf{Nuclear baseload.}
    Nuclear units are always online (must-run) but may modulate output within capacity and ramp limits ($R^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z = 0.5\,\overline{P}^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z$), reflecting limited short-term flexibility while allowing moderate intra-day adjustment.

  \item \textbf{Renewable curtailment.}
    Solar and wind generation equal available power minus a non-negative curtailment variable, penalized in the objective.

  \item \textbf{Demand response.}
    DR is modelled with $K$ cost tiers of increasing marginal cost, big-M activation constraints linking the binary~$\delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}$ to continuous shedding, a maximum number of activation events per zone, a rebound energy backlog with exponential decay that must clear by the end of the horizon, and inter-temporal ramp limits.

  \item \textbf{Battery storage.}
    The mode binary~$\sigma^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t}$ enforces mutual exclusivity of charge and discharge. State-of-charge evolution accounts for asymmetric round-trip efficiencies and self-discharge, with end-of-horizon SOC bounded within a tolerance band around the initial level.

  \item \textbf{Reservoir hydro.}
    Reservoir level evolves with natural inflows minus release and spillage, both bounded by capacity.

  \item \textbf{Pumped-hydro storage.}
    Mirrors the battery formulation with its own mode binary, round-trip efficiencies, self-discharge retention, and final level bounds.

  \item \textbf{Transmission and cross-border exchanges.}
    Line flows are bounded by capacity (bidirectional). Cross-border import and export are mutually exclusive via the mode binary~$\sigma^{\mathrm{imp}}_t$ and bounded by aggregate interconnection capacity.

  \item \textbf{Nodal power balance.}
    At every zone and time step, total generation plus demand response, unserved energy, and net inflows must equal demand plus storage charging, outflows, and over-generation spillage. Cross-border exchanges enter the balance at a single designated anchor zone.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Variable Fixing and Solver Strategy}
\label{sec:milp_fixing}

To tighten the formulation and reduce solve time, the model pre-fixes binary variables for inactive assets: thermal commitment is fixed to zero in zones with no thermal capacity, DR activation flags are fixed where DR capacity is zero, and storage mode variables are fixed for zones without the corresponding storage asset. Similarly, the import mode binary is fixed when no cross-border capacity exists. This pre-processing eliminates unnecessary branching and typically reduces the effective binary count by 30--60\%, depending on scenario sparsity.

Each scenario is solved with a configurable time limit ($\overline{T}_{\mathrm{solve}}$). Instances that terminate within the time limit yield optimal primal solutions, are labeled \emph{gold labels} and will be used for pretraining of the EBM; those that time out are classified as \emph{silver labels} and represent the best incumbent feasible found within the time limit. These incumbents will only be used for further training of the EBM.

\subsubsection{LP Relaxation and Dual Information}
\label{sec:milp_duals}

As part of the oracle's data-generation pass, the same unit-commitment model is also solved a second time with all binary variables relaxed to their continuous $[0,1]$ counterparts. This LP relaxation serves two purposes: (i)~it provides a lower bound on the optimal cost (the integrality gap is stored alongside each scenario), and (ii)~it extracts dual variables (shadow prices) for key constraints---in particular, the nodal power-balance duals yield locational marginal prices, and the transmission-capacity and storage-level duals reveal congestion rents and storage shadow values.

Note that this LP relaxation is distinct from the conditional LP worker used later in the pipeline (Section~5) to recover continuous dispatch after the EBM sampler has predicted binary decisions.


\subsubsection{Model Size and Complexity}
\label{sec:milp_size}
These structural properties—binary explosion, strong inter-temporal coupling, and tight end-of-horizon constraints—are known to severely limit decomposition and to challenge branch-and-bound methods, especially under high stress scenarios.
Indeed, the number of decision variables and constraints scales with the product of zones, time steps, and asset types. For a scenario with $|\mathcal{Z}|$ zones, $|\mathcal{T}|$ time periods, $|\mathcal{L}|$ lines, and $K$ DR tiers, the approximate model dimensions are:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Binary variables:} $\sim 4\,|\mathcal{Z}|\,|\mathcal{T}| + |\mathcal{T}|$ (thermal commitment and startup, DR activation, battery and pumped-hydro modes, plus import mode), before pre-fixing.
  \item \textbf{Continuous variables:} $\sim (14 + K)\,|\mathcal{Z}|\,|\mathcal{T}| + |\mathcal{L}|\,|\mathcal{T}| + 2\,|\mathcal{T}|$.
  \item \textbf{Constraints:} $\sim (16 + 2K)\,|\mathcal{Z}|\,|\mathcal{T}| + 2\,|\mathcal{L}|\,|\mathcal{T}| + 4\,|\mathcal{Z}| + 2\,|\mathcal{T}|$.
\end{itemize}
In our benchmark suite (Table~\ref{tab:scenario-comparison}), this ranges from ${\sim}\,45\,000$ variables and ${\sim}\,58\,000$ constraints for simple scenarios to ${\sim}\,139\,000$ variables and ${\sim}\,181\,000$ constraints for critical ones, confirming that repeated solves across thousands of scenarios quickly become the computational bottleneck that motivates the downstream learning pipeline.

\section{Representation learning}
\label{sec:graph_encoder}

A key design choice of the proposed pipeline is to \emph{not} flatten each scenario into a fixed-length feature vector. Doing so would destroy the spatial topology, discard the variable-size structure (the number of zones, regions, and assets changes from one scenario to the next), and ignore the relational constraints that are the very source of MILP complexity. Instead, we project every scenario onto a \emph{multi-layer heterogeneous temporal graph} and learn an encoder that produces compact embeddings while respecting the containment hierarchy and temporal couplings.

This section gives the motivation and high-level design of the two components: graph construction (Section~\ref{sec:graph_projection}) and the Hierarchical Temporal Encoder (Section~\ref{sec:hte}). Formal definitions, complete edge-type catalogues, and architectural equations are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:graph_hte_details}.

\subsection{Projection onto Multi-Layer Heterogeneous Temporal Graphs}
\label{sec:graph_projection}

\subsubsection{Why a graph?}

Energy system scenarios are naturally hierarchical: a single nation contains several regions, each region groups multiple load zones, and each zone owns a portfolio of generation and storage assets. These relationships---together with the transmission network that connects zones and the weather patterns that drive renewable output---are most faithfully captured by a graph whose structure mirrors the physical system. A graph representation offers three advantages over tabular alternatives:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Variable size.} The number of zones, assets, and inter-ties varies across scenarios. A graph accommodates this naturally, whereas a flat vector requires padding or truncation that introduces artefacts.
  \item \textbf{Explicit topology.} Containment (nation $\to$ region $\to$ zone $\to$ asset), transmission lines, and weather influence are encoded as typed edges, giving the downstream encoder direct access to the relational structure that governs MILP feasibility.
  \item \textbf{Temporal coupling.} By replicating the spatial graph at every time step and adding inter-temporal edges (state-of-charge continuity, ramping limits, demand-response cooldowns), the graph encodes the same coupling constraints that make the MILP difficult to solve.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Graph structure at a glance}

Each scenario is mapped to a heterogeneous attributed graph $\mathcal{G}(s)$ with five node types and eleven edge types (seven spatial, four temporal). The construction is fully determined by the scenario specification and requires no MILP solution, so the same builder can be used at inference time for unseen scenarios.

\paragraph{Nodes.}
The graph contains one \emph{nation} node (system-wide aggregates), one \emph{region} node per geographical region, one \emph{zone} node per load zone, one \emph{asset} node per active technology instance, and one \emph{weather} node per region. Asset nodes are attached to the appropriate spatial level: zonal technologies (thermal, solar, wind, battery, run-of-river hydro, demand response) are children of their parent zone, while large-scale resources defined at the regional level (nuclear, reservoir hydro, pumped hydro) are children of their parent region. An asset node is only created when the corresponding capacity is strictly positive, so the graph size varies across scenarios.

\paragraph{Spatial edges.}
Containment edges form a strict tree (nation $\to$ regions $\to$ zones $\to$ zonal assets, plus region $\to$ regional assets). Weather-influence edges connect each weather cell to the zones and renewable assets in its region. Bidirectional transmission edges link zones that share a power line, carrying the line's thermal capacity and electrical distance as attributes.

\paragraph{Temporal extension (supra-graph).}
To capture inter-temporal MILP constraints, each base node is replicated at every time step~$t \in \{0,\dots,T{-}1\}$, and directed temporal edges are added between consecutive copies of the same asset: \emph{SOC edges} for storage assets, \emph{ramp edges} for thermal and nuclear generators, and \emph{cooldown edges} for demand-response units. The result is a \emph{supra-graph} whose size scales as $N_{\mathrm{base}} \times T$. Sinusoidal positional encodings are concatenated to node features to provide periodicity-aware time information.

The complete schema---node feature dimensions, all eleven edge types, and temporal edge construction rules---is given in Appendix~\ref{app:graph_schema}. \subsection{Hierarchical Temporal Encoder}
\label{sec:hte}

\subsubsection{Why not a standard graph neural network?}

The supra-graph constructed above can contain a very large number of nodes. Applying a standard dense-attention graph Transformer to such inputs would require $\mathcal{O}(N^2 T^2)$ memory---prohibitive even on modern GPUs. At the same time, a shallow message-passing network that treats all nodes uniformly would fail to distinguish between local asset-level interactions (e.g., a battery's SOC evolution within a single zone) and global system-level patterns (e.g., the national demand--supply balance over a day). The encoder must therefore be \emph{hierarchy-aware}: it should aggregate local information upward, reason about temporal dynamics at the system level, and then distribute this global context back to the fine-grained nodes that need it.

\subsubsection{Three-phase architecture}

We introduce a \emph{Hierarchical Temporal Encoder} (HTE) that factorises computation into three phases, mirroring the natural information flow in power system operations:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Bottom-up spatial encoding.} Information flows upward through the containment hierarchy: asset-level representations are aggregated into zones, zones into regions, and regions into a single nation-level summary. At each level, graph attention layers~\citep{brody_how_2022} allow neighbouring nodes to exchange information (e.g., assets sharing a zone, zones connected by transmission lines) before pooling. Skip connections preserve local detail at every level for later use.

  \item \textbf{Temporal Transformer at the nation level.} Once the entire system state has been compressed into a single node per time step, a standard Transformer encoder~\citep{vaswani2017attention} captures temporal dependencies across the horizon. Because the sequence length equals the number of time steps ($T = 24$ in the benchmark), dense self-attention is computationally negligible, yet it has access to the full spatial context at each step.

  \item \textbf{Top-down decoding.} The temporally enriched nation representation is broadcast back through the hierarchy: first to regions, then to zones, then to assets. At each level, the broadcast signal is combined with the skip connection saved during the bottom-up phase, so that the final embeddings fuse global temporal context with local spatial detail.
\end{enumerate}

The output is a set of temporal embedding vectors at every hierarchical level---assets, zones, regions, and nation---each of dimension $D = 128$. The zone-level embeddings are the primary conditioning signal consumed by the downstream EBM (Section~4), while the asset-level embeddings drive the self-supervised training objective.


\subsubsection{Self-supervised training}
\label{sec:hte_training}

A distinguishing feature of the HTE is that it is trained \emph{without any MILP solution labels}. The training signal comes entirely from the structure of the scenario graphs themselves, through a contrastive learning objective.

\paragraph{Intuition.}
The key idea is that the embedding of a given asset at time~$t$ should be similar to the embedding of \emph{the same asset a few time steps later} (a ``positive pair''), and dissimilar to embeddings of \emph{unrelated assets at random times} (``negative pairs''). This encourages the encoder to learn representations that capture the temporal dynamics of the system---ramping patterns, charge/discharge cycles, demand response activations---without needing to see any optimal dispatch solution.

\paragraph{Multi-lag design.}
Rather than using a single lag (e.g., $t \to t{+}1$), we form positive pairs at three lags: $\tau \in \{1, 4, 8\}$ hours. This ensures the encoder captures dynamics at multiple time scales: short-range (hourly ramping and SOC transitions), medium-range (intra-day patterns), and longer-range (diurnal cycles). The loss is the InfoNCE objective~\citep{oord2018representation}, a standard contrastive loss widely used in self-supervised representation learning. Because training uses the asset level---the finest granularity---the loss implicitly shapes all coarser levels through the pooling and skip-connection pathways.

\paragraph{Training setup.}
The model is trained on 4\,000 scenario graphs (with 1\,000 additional scenarios held out for validation) using the AdamW optimiser~\citep{loshchilov2019decoupled} with cosine learning-rate annealing and early stopping. Because graph sizes vary widely ($N_{\mathrm{base}} \in [33, 851]$), only one graph fits in GPU memory at a time; gradient accumulation over 8~steps provides an effective batch size of~8. On a single NVIDIA H100 GPU, training converges in ${\sim}\,80$ epochs (${\sim}\,8$~hours); generating embeddings for all $5\,000$ scenarios takes an additional hour.

The complete architectural equations, layer-by-layer specifications, and the full hyperparameter table are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:hte_details}, along with an architecture diagram (Figure~\ref{fig:hte_architecture}).

\section{Energy-Based Learning}
\label{sec:ebm}

The encoder of Section~\ref{sec:graph_encoder} compresses each scenario into zone-level temporal embeddings $h \in \mathbb{R}^{Z \times T \times D}$. These embeddings capture \emph{what the system looks like}---topology, asset portfolio, weather, demand---but they say nothing about \emph{what to do}. The present section introduces the component that bridges this gap: a conditional energy-based model (EBM) that learns an energy landscape over the binary commitment and activation decisions defined in Section~\ref{sec:milp_variables}, and a Langevin sampler that explores this landscape to produce high-quality decision candidates in a fraction of the time required by a MILP solver.

\subsection{Why an Energy-Based Model?}
\label{sec:ebm_why}

The binary decisions that drive MILP complexity---thermal commitment, storage charge/discharge modes, demand-response activation---form a structured, high-dimensional discrete space whose feasible region is shaped by inter-temporal and inter-zonal constraints. Three properties of this space make a standard supervised classifier inadequate and motivate the EBM formulation:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Multimodality.} For a given scenario, multiple distinct commitment schedules may achieve near-optimal costs (e.g., shifting a thermal start-up by one period, or substituting battery discharge for demand response). A classifier predicts a single output; an EBM assigns a \emph{scalar energy} to every candidate, naturally representing a landscape with multiple low-energy basins.

  \item \textbf{Controlled exploration.} Rather than committing to one prediction, we want to \emph{sample} several candidates and select the best after feasibility repair and LP evaluation. Langevin dynamics provides a principled, gradient-guided mechanism to draw diverse samples from the learned distribution, with tunable temperature and step count to trade off exploration breadth against sample quality.

  \item \textbf{Compatibility with downstream repair.} The sampler produces \emph{relaxed} decisions in $(0,1)$ during training (for gradient flow) and \emph{binary} decisions in $\{0,1\}$ at inference (for the feasibility decoder and LP worker). This dual-mode design means the EBM does not need to enforce hard constraints itself---it only needs to place low energy near feasible, low-cost regions, while the decoder (Section~5) handles the rest.
\end{enumerate}

In short, the EBM acts as a \emph{learned prior over binary decisions}, conditioned on the scenario embedding. It does not replace the MILP; it compresses the MILP's implicit knowledge about good commitment patterns into a fast, differentiable energy function that can be queried thousands of times per second.

\paragraph{Why not alternative generative architectures?}
Several classes of models could, in principle, generate binary commitment candidates. We briefly justify the EBM choice against the main alternatives:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Binary classifier / regression.} A feed-forward network that directly predicts $u^* \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times 7}$ from the embedding $h$ would produce a single point estimate, forfeiting the multi-candidate diversity that is central to our pipeline (best-of-$K$ selection, solution-pool hedging). Moreover, it would require differentiable relaxation or straight-through estimators for training, adding complexity without the principled energy landscape that enables gradient-guided exploration.

  \item \textbf{Autoregressive model.} An autoregressive decoder (e.g., a Transformer generating one $u_{z,t,f}$ at a time) would naturally handle multimodality via ancestral sampling. However, the generation order is arbitrary for our structured grid ($Z \times T \times 7$), and sequential decoding would be orders of magnitude slower than parallel Langevin sampling---especially for large scenarios with $>100$ zones. The EBM evaluates all decisions simultaneously in a single forward pass.

  \item \textbf{Diffusion model.} Score-based diffusion models share the iterative refinement philosophy of Langevin dynamics and have shown strong results in continuous domains. However, they require a fixed noise schedule and many denoising steps (typically 50--1000) to produce high-quality samples. Our Langevin sampler achieves competitive results with 100 steps and offers explicit control over the exploration--exploitation trade-off via temperature annealing, without the overhead of learning a separate score network at each noise level. The EBM's energy function also provides a direct, interpretable quality signal (lower energy $\approx$ better schedule) that the downstream LP worker can exploit for candidate ranking.

  \item \textbf{Variational autoencoder (VAE).} A conditional VAE would require defining a meaningful latent space for commitment schedules and training an encoder--decoder pair. The latent bottleneck risks losing the fine-grained temporal structure of start-up sequences and storage cycles. The EBM avoids this by operating directly in the decision space, with the GRU backbone preserving temporal coherence.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The EBM's combination of parallel evaluation, multi-candidate sampling, and compatibility with the reconstruct-then-optimise pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:reconstruction}) makes it the most natural fit for our setting.


\subsection{Decision Representation}
\label{sec:ebm_features}

Each MILP solution is projected onto a binary tensor $u \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times F}$ with $F = 7$ features per zone per time step:
\begin{equation}
u_{z,t} = \bigl[\,
  \underbrace{b^{\mathrm{ch}}_{z,t},\; b^{\mathrm{dis}}_{z,t}}_{\text{battery}},\;
  \underbrace{g^{\mathrm{ch}}_{z,t},\; g^{\mathrm{dis}}_{z,t}}_{\text{pumped}},\;
  \underbrace{\delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}}_{\text{DR}},\;
  \underbrace{v^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t},\; u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}}_{\text{thermal}}
\,\bigr].
\label{eq:ebm_features}
\end{equation}
The first four entries encode storage mode decisions (charge/discharge activity for batteries and pumped hydro), the fifth is the demand-response activation flag, the sixth is the thermal start-up indicator (off$\to$on transition), and the seventh is the thermal commitment status. This compact representation captures precisely the discrete decisions that create MILP hardness, while continuous dispatch variables (power levels, SOC trajectories, flows) are left to the LP worker.

Because scenarios have different numbers of zones, batches are padded to the maximum zone count and a binary zone mask $m \in \{0,1\}^{B \times Z_{\max}}$ ensures that padded zones contribute neither to the energy nor to the sampler gradients.


\subsection{Energy Model Architecture}
\label{sec:ebm_model}

The energy function $E_\theta(u \mid h)$ must satisfy two requirements: (i)~it must process the temporal structure of commitment trajectories (e.g., start-up sequences, charge/discharge cycles), and (ii)~it must handle variable zone counts without introducing artefacts from padding.

\paragraph{Trajectory-level processing.}
At each zone, the binary decision vector $u_{z,t}$ is concatenated with the corresponding HTE embedding $h_{z,t}$ and projected to a hidden representation. A bidirectional GRU then processes the full temporal trajectory of each zone, producing a sequence of context-aware hidden states. An energy head (a small MLP) maps each hidden state to a per-zone, per-timestep energy contribution $e_{z,t}$.

\paragraph{Aggregation with peak term.}
The scalar energy for the entire sample is a weighted combination of two terms: a \emph{mean energy} that captures the average quality of the commitment schedule, and a \emph{peak energy} (a soft-max over timesteps via log-sum-exp) that penalises the worst-case period. The peak term is critical for power-system schedules, where a single period of infeasibility---a missed start-up during peak demand, or an empty battery during a wind lull---can dominate the cost. Both terms are computed with zone masking to exclude padded zones.

\paragraph{Energy bounding.}
To prevent unbounded energy magnitudes during training, the raw energy is passed through a scaled $\tanh$ nonlinearity: $E = E_{\max} \cdot \tanh(E_{\mathrm{raw}} / E_{\max})$, where $E_{\max}$ is a hyperparameter (set to 50 in our experiments). This soft clamp stabilises contrastive divergence training without distorting the energy landscape near the origin.

The full architectural specification---layer dimensions, initialisation, and the masked log-sum-exp formulation---is given in Appendix~\ref{app:ebm_details}.


\subsection{Langevin Sampling in Logit Space}
\label{sec:ebm_sampler}

\subsubsection{The key idea}

Since the decision variables are binary, standard Langevin dynamics (which assumes a continuous domain) cannot be applied directly. We address this by operating in an unconstrained \emph{logit space}: each binary variable $u_{z,t,f}$ is parameterised as $u = \sigma(z)$ where $z \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function. Langevin dynamics runs over the logits $z$, and the sigmoid maps the result back to probabilities in $(0,1)$. At inference time, these probabilities are binarised via Bernoulli sampling to produce the discrete decisions consumed by the feasibility decoder.

\subsubsection{Update rule}

At each Langevin step $k$, the logits are updated as:
\begin{equation}
z^{(k+1)} \;\leftarrow\; z^{(k)} - \eta\,\nabla_z E_\theta\!\bigl(\sigma(z^{(k)}),\, h\bigr) + \sigma_k \sqrt{\eta}\;\varepsilon_k, \qquad \varepsilon_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I),
\label{eq:langevin_update}
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is the step size and $\sigma_k$ is the noise scale at step~$k$. The gradient $\nabla_z E$ is computed via backpropagation through the sigmoid and the energy network, and is normalised by its standard deviation across dimensions to prevent any single zone or feature from dominating the update. The noise term injects stochasticity to avoid local minima and enable diverse sampling.

\subsubsection{Temperature annealing}

A temperature schedule linearly decays $\sigma_k$ from a high initial value $\sigma_{\max}$ (broad exploration) to a low final value $\sigma_{\min}$ (local refinement) over the course of the Langevin chain. This annealing is analogous to simulated annealing and ensures that the sampler first explores the energy landscape broadly before converging to a low-energy basin. Importantly, this temperature is distinct from any temperature in the energy model itself.

\subsubsection{Train vs.\ inference modes}

The sampler operates in two modes:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Train mode:} returns relaxed $u \in (0,1)^{Z \times T \times F}$, so that the energy and its gradients remain differentiable. Fewer Langevin steps are used (10--50, ramped via a curriculum) to keep training cost manageable.
  \item \textbf{Infer mode:} returns binary $u \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times F}$ after binarisation of the final relaxed output. More steps (100) and lower noise produce sharper, higher-quality samples.
\end{itemize}

The complete sampler specification (initialisation, prior drift, curriculum schedule) is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:ebm_details}.


\subsection{Two-Step Training}
\label{sec:ebm_training}

Training proceeds in two steps that mirror the gold/silver label split introduced in Section~\ref{sec:milp_fixing}: a \emph{gold pre-training} phase that teaches the energy function to distinguish optimal from random schedules, followed by a \emph{silver fine-tuning} phase that aligns the energy landscape with economic cost by integrating feedback from the LP worker.

\subsubsection{Step A: Gold pre-training (contrastive divergence)}

The goal is to shape the energy landscape so that MILP-optimal binary schedules $u^*$ sit in low-energy regions, while arbitrary or random schedules receive high energy.

\paragraph{Loss function.}
Training uses contrastive divergence (CD): at each iteration, the model evaluates the energy of a \emph{positive sample} $u^+$ (the MILP solution) and a \emph{negative sample} $u^-$ (produced by the sampler), and minimises:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CD}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{u^+ \sim p_{\mathrm{data}}}\!\bigl[E_\theta(u^+, h)\bigr]}_{\text{push down positives}} - \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{u^- \sim p_\theta}\!\bigl[E_\theta(u^-, h)\bigr]}_{\text{push up negatives}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{reg}} \, R(E),
\label{eq:cd_loss}
\end{equation}
where $R(E)$ is a soft regulariser that penalises energy magnitudes exceeding a threshold, preventing the well-known energy-scale drift of EBM training.


\paragraph{Curriculum on sampler strength.}
The number of Langevin steps used to generate negatives is linearly ramped from a small initial value (10~steps) to a larger final value (50~steps) over the course of training. This curriculum ensures that the sampler (the ``adversary'' in contrastive learning) does not overwhelm the model early on, but becomes progressively stronger as the model improves.



\subsubsection{Step B: Silver fine-tuning (preference learning)}

Gold pre-training teaches the EBM to separate optimal from arbitrary schedules, but it does not directly optimise for economic cost. Step~B closes this gap by introducing a \emph{preference loss} that leverages the LP worker to rank candidate solutions by cost.

\paragraph{Generating preference pairs.}
Periodically during training, the sampler generates multiple binary candidates for a subset of scenarios. Each candidate is evaluated by the LP worker (Section~5), which fixes the binaries and solves for the optimal continuous dispatch, yielding a total operating cost. This produces \emph{preference pairs} $(u_a, u_b)$ where $u_a$ has lower LP cost than $u_b$.

\paragraph{Preference loss.}
The EBM is trained so that the better candidate receives lower energy, via a margin ranking loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pref}} = \max\!\bigl(0,\; E_\theta(u_a, h) - E_\theta(u_b, h) + \delta\bigr),
\label{eq:pref_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\delta > 0$ is a margin. This loss directly aligns the energy landscape with economic optimality: lower energy $\Leftrightarrow$ lower operating cost.

\paragraph{Combined objective.}
The silver training loss combines contrastive divergence (to maintain the energy gap) with the preference term (to align with cost):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{silver}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{CD}} \, \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CD}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{pref}} \, \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pref}}.
\label{eq:silver_loss}
\end{equation}
Silver fine-tuning uses only the silver-tier scenarios (feasible MILP incumbents that timed out before proving optimality), so that the gold-tier data remains uncontaminated for validation. A separate Langevin curriculum and a lower learning rate prevent catastrophic forgetting of the pre-trained energy landscape.

\subsection{From Samples to Feasible Plans}
\label{sec:ebm_inference}

At inference time, the trained EBM and sampler produce binary decision candidates that are passed to the downstream feasibility decoder and LP worker (detailed in Section~5). The full inference path for a new scenario is:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Encode.} Build the heterogeneous temporal graph and run the HTE to obtain zone-level embeddings $h \in \mathbb{R}^{Z \times T \times D}$.
  \item \textbf{Sample.} Run the Langevin sampler in inference mode (100 steps, low noise, temperature annealing) conditioned on $h$. Multiple independent chains can be run in parallel on GPU to produce a pool of diverse candidates.
  \item \textbf{Binarise.} Bernoulli-sample the relaxed output to obtain $\hat{u} \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times 7}$.
  \item \textbf{Decode and dispatch.} The feasibility decoder repairs constraint violations (Section~5), and the LP worker recovers continuous dispatch variables, yielding a complete operating plan with an associated cost.
  \item \textbf{Select.} If multiple candidates were generated, select the one with the lowest LP cost.
\end{enumerate}

This pipeline replaces the combinatorial search of branch-and-bound with a sequence of differentiable forward passes (steps~1--3) followed by $N_s$ parallel decode-and-solve passes (step~4, one per Langevin candidate; $N_s{=}5$ in our experiments). All reported pipeline times therefore include $5\times$ the decoder and LP cost. Despite this multiplicative factor, the pipeline targets orders-of-magnitude speedup for large scenario sweeps while preserving solution quality through the feasibility-aware post-processing stages.

\section{Solution Reconstruction}
\label{sec:reconstruction}

The EBM and Langevin sampler of Section~\ref{sec:ebm} produce binary decision candidates $\hat{u} \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times 7}$ that encode commitment and mode schedules for every zone and time step. However, these candidates are \emph{not} directly usable as power-system dispatch plans: they may violate physical constraints (e.g., ramping limits, SOC bounds), they say nothing about the continuous variables (power levels, storage trajectories, flows), and nothing guarantees that supply equals demand at every node. The present section describes the two components that bridge this gap: a \emph{feasibility decoder} that repairs binary decisions into a physically consistent dispatch skeleton, and a \emph{two-stage LP worker} that recovers optimal continuous variables while preserving the repaired binaries.

Together, these components form a \emph{reconstruct-then-optimise} pipeline: the decoder provides a fast, heuristic feasibility projection (milliseconds), while the LP worker performs an exact optimisation of the continuous sub-problem (seconds). This decomposition exploits the classical insight that, once the discrete decisions are fixed, the remaining unit-commitment problem reduces to a linear program that can be solved efficiently and to global optimality.


\subsection{Feasibility Decoder}
\label{sec:feasibility_decoder}

The feasibility decoder takes a binary candidate $\hat{u}$ from the EBM and the scenario physics (capacities, demand, weather, storage parameters) and produces a \emph{feasible plan}: a complete set of binary and continuous decisions that satisfies power balance, storage dynamics, and operational limits at every zone and time step.

\subsubsection{Design philosophy}

The decoder is \emph{not} an optimiser---it is a fast, greedy projection that transforms an approximate EBM sample into a physically realisable dispatch. It follows two guiding principles:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Trust the EBM first.} When the EBM suggests that a resource should be active (e.g., thermal on, battery discharging), the decoder dispatches that resource \emph{before} falling back to a merit-order heuristic. This ensures that the learned commitment patterns---which encode implicit knowledge about startup costs, storage cycling, and demand-response timing---are preserved as much as possible.

  \item \textbf{Guarantee feasibility always.} If the EBM suggestions are insufficient to balance supply and demand, the decoder activates additional resources in merit order (cheapest first for deficit, least valuable first for surplus). As a last resort, unserved energy or overgeneration spillage absorb any remaining imbalance.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Three-phase dispatch}

The decoder processes one time step at a time, tracking storage state-of-charge and thermal dispatch history across the horizon. At each time step, it executes three phases:

\paragraph{Phase~1: Must-run and free resources.}
Nuclear baseload and available renewables (solar, wind, run-of-river hydro) are dispatched first, as they have zero or near-zero marginal cost. The residual demand after this phase determines whether the system is in deficit or surplus.

\paragraph{Phase~2: Deficit handling (EBM-guided, then merit-order fallback).}
If the residual is positive (demand exceeds must-run supply), the decoder dispatches resources in the following priority:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Nuclear headroom (remaining capacity beyond the must-run minimum).
  \item Reservoir hydro (if water is available).
  \item \emph{EBM-suggested} storage discharge (pumped hydro, then battery)---only if the EBM flagged the corresponding mode as active.
  \item \emph{EBM-suggested} thermal generation, subject to ramp-rate limits and minimum stable generation.
  \item \emph{Fallback}: any storage or thermal capacity \emph{not} suggested by the EBM, dispatched in the same merit order.
  \item Cross-border imports.
  \item Demand response (subject to a maximum activation duration over the horizon).
  \item Unserved energy (last resort, heavily penalised in the cost function).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Phase~3: Surplus handling.}
If the residual is negative (supply exceeds demand), excess is absorbed in the following priority:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Cross-border exports (restricted to the anchor zone, matching the MILP model).
  \item Thermal reduction (respecting ramp-down limits and minimum stable generation).
  \item Renewable curtailment (wind first, then solar).
  \item \emph{EBM-suggested} storage charging (pumped hydro, then battery), followed by fallback charging.
  \item Overgeneration spillage (last resort).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{MILP-consistent physics}

The decoder mirrors the constraint families of the full MILP (Appendix~\ref{app:milp_constraints}) as closely as a greedy heuristic allows:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Storage dynamics.} Separate charge and discharge efficiencies ($\eta^+$, $\eta^-$) and per-period self-discharge retention ($\rho$), matching the MILP's SOC evolution equation~\eqref{eq:battery_soc}.
  \item \textbf{Thermal ramp limits.} Dispatch at time~$t$ is clamped to $[p_{t-1} - R, \, p_{t-1} + R]$, with a startup exception that allows jumping directly to $\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}$ from zero.
  \item \textbf{Anchor-zone exports.} Only the designated anchor zone may export, while imports are distributed across all zones as a proxy for network flows.
  \item \textbf{Thermal minimum generation.} If a thermal unit is committed, its dispatch is forced to at least $\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}$ to avoid the infeasible region between 0 and $\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}$.
\end{itemize}

The full algorithmic specification, including storage SOC tracking, DR duration accounting, and the zone-by-zone greedy loop, is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:reconstruction_details}.


\subsection{Two-Stage LP Worker}
\label{sec:lp_worker}

The feasibility decoder produces a physically consistent dispatch skeleton, but the resulting continuous variables (power levels, SOC trajectories, flows) are only approximately optimal. The LP worker recovers the \emph{true} economic optimum by solving the original MILP model with all binary variables fixed to the decoder's values, reducing the problem to a linear program.

\subsubsection{Why a two-stage approach?}

Fixing all binaries and solving the resulting LP is fast ($<20$\,s) and sufficient for the vast majority of scenarios. However, the decoder is a heuristic: it may occasionally produce binary schedules that are \emph{technically feasible} in isolation but lead to \emph{tight or infeasible LP sub-problems} when the full MILP constraint set is enforced (e.g., a thermal commitment pattern that violates startup-shutdown coupling, or a storage schedule that overshoots the final SOC tolerance). Rather than discarding these solutions, we employ a progressive repair strategy that selectively relaxes the most problematic binaries while staying as close as possible to the decoder's original suggestion.

\subsubsection{Stage progression}

The LP worker proceeds through up to five stages, stopping as soon as the solution quality is acceptable (total slack $\leq$ tolerance):

\paragraph{Stage~1: Hard-fix LP.}
All binary variables (thermal commitment, storage modes, DR activation) are fixed to the decoder's values. The remaining integer variables (e.g., import/export mode) are relaxed to continuous, yielding a pure LP. If the total slack (unserved energy + overgeneration) is below the tolerance, the solution is accepted immediately. This stage succeeds for the majority of scenarios and runs in $<20$\,s.

\paragraph{Stage~2: Repair-20.}
If Stage~1 produces excessive slack, the LP worker identifies the $K{=}20$ most critical (zone, time) pairs---scored by slack magnitude and thermal tension (capacity mismatch between the fixed commitment and the optimal dispatch). Binary variables at these indices are unfixed across \emph{all} variable families (thermal, storage, DR), and a deviation penalty discourages the solver from straying far from the decoder's suggestions. A flip-budget constraint (10\% of total binaries) limits the total number of changes.

\paragraph{Stage~3: Repair-100.}
Same structure as Stage~2, but with $K{=}100$ critical indices and a larger flip budget (50\% of total binaries), allowing more substantial repairs.

\paragraph{Stage~4: Full soft.}
If targeted repair is insufficient, all binaries are unfixed and relaxed to continuous $[0,1]$, with a soft deviation penalty on all (zone, time) pairs to anchor the solution near the decoder's suggestion. This stage always finds a feasible solution but may produce fractional binaries.

\paragraph{Stage~5: Round and refix.}
The fractional solution from Stage~4 is rounded ($u \geq 0.5 \to 1$, else $0$) across all six binary variable families (thermal commitment, thermal startup, battery mode, pumped-storage mode, demand-response activation, and import/export mode) and used as a new set of fixed binaries for a final hard-fix LP. The Stage~5 result is \emph{always} used, even if its slack is higher than Stage~4's, because Stage~4's fractional binaries do not constitute a valid integer-feasible dispatch plan. This guarantees that every pipeline solution has fully integral binary variables.

\subsubsection{Deviation penalty and flip budget}

Two mechanisms keep the LP worker's repairs close to the decoder's intent:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Deviation penalty.} For each unfixed binary $u_{z,t}$, auxiliary variables $d^+_{z,t}, d^-_{z,t} \geq 0$ are introduced such that $u_{z,t} - \hat{u}_{z,t} = d^+_{z,t} - d^-_{z,t}$, and the objective is augmented with $\lambda (d^+ + d^-)$, where $\lambda$ is a large penalty weight. This soft constraint encourages the solver to keep $u$ at the decoder's target unless doing so would cause infeasibility.

  \item \textbf{Flip budget.} A hard constraint $\sum_{z,t} (d^+_{z,t} + d^-_{z,t}) \leq B$ limits the total number of binary flips, ensuring that the LP worker makes only localised corrections rather than wholesale restructuring of the commitment schedule.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{End-to-End Reconstruction Cost}
\label{sec:reconstruction_cost}

The combined decoder + LP worker pipeline is designed for throughput. The feasibility decoder runs in pure Python/PyTorch with no external solver calls, completing in $<100$\,ms per scenario. The LP worker's Stage~1 (the common case) solves a continuous LP with the same constraint structure as the original MILP but no branch-and-bound, typically completing in seconds depending on scenario size. Even in the rare cases requiring Stages~2--5, the total reconstruction time remains well below the MILP solve time (which can reach minutes to hours for hard scenarios), because the LP worker never re-solves the full combinatorial problem---it only adjusts a small number of binary variables around the decoder's suggestion.

This asymmetry is the key design insight: the \emph{hard} combinatorial decisions are handled by the fast EBM + decoder pipeline, while the \emph{easy} continuous optimisation is delegated to a mature LP solver. The result is a solution path that combines the speed of learned heuristics with the exactness of mathematical programming.

\section{Experimental Setup and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section evaluates the full GNN--EBM pipeline against the MILP oracle across 300 scenarios spanning three criticality families. The goal is twofold: (i) quantify how solution quality and runtime scale as the scenario ``complexity knob'' is increased, and (ii) assess whether the pipeline delivers a practical advantage for large-scale flexibility exploration, where hundreds to thousands of scenarios must be solved under time pressure.


\subsection{Evaluation Scenarios}
\label{sec:eval_scenarios}

\subsubsection{Criticality-stratified test families}

The evaluation set is constructed independently from the 5\,000-scenario training corpus described in Section~\ref{sec:scenario_generator}. Three families of 100 scenarios each are generated by biasing the scenario generator's sampling space toward distinct criticality regimes (Section~\ref{sec:criticality}):

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Low criticality} ($\bar{C} = 0.33$, range $[0.29, 0.34]$). Small topologies (4--20 zones), moderate demand ($\text{demand\_scale} \in [0.7, 0.95]$), few binary variables ($<10\,000$). MILP solves in under 2\,s.
  \item \textbf{Medium criticality} ($\bar{C} = 0.41$, range $[0.41, 0.42]$). Medium topologies (30--80 zones), elevated demand ($\text{demand\_scale} \in [1.05, 1.25]$), moderate binary count. MILP solves in 1--120\,s.
  \item \textbf{High criticality} ($\bar{C} = 0.49$, range $[0.48, 0.51]$). Large topologies (80--160 zones), stressed demand ($\text{demand\_scale} \in [1.3, 1.6]$), many binary variables ($>30\,000$). MILP solves in tens of seconds to hours; some instances hit the solver time limit.
\end{itemize}

The two dominant hardness drivers---identified from empirical analysis of 5\,000 training scenarios (Cohen's $d > 1.0$)---are \emph{problem size} (number of zones, assets, binary variables) and \emph{demand stress} (demand scale factor, peak-to-valley ratio). Economic parameters (CO$_2$ price, fuel costs) have negligible discriminative power ($d < 0.1$) and are sampled from moderate default ranges across all families.

Each scenario is solved to optimality (or time limit) by the MILP oracle to produce a reference objective value. Crucially, none of these 300 evaluation scenarios were seen during EBM training, ensuring a genuine out-of-sample assessment.

\subsubsection{Hardware and software}

All pipeline evaluations (graph construction, HTE encoding, EBM sampling, feasibility decoding, LP solving) were executed on a single Google Colab instance equipped with an NVIDIA A100-SXM4-40GB GPU and a single-threaded HiGHS LP solver (via Pyomo's APPSI interface). The MILP oracle was solved with HiGHS using default settings and a 10-minute time limit. All models use PyTorch~2.5.1 with CUDA~12.4.


\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:eval_protocol}

For each of the 300 evaluation scenarios, the pipeline executes the full inference path described in Section~\ref{sec:ebm_inference}:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Build the heterogeneous temporal graph from the scenario JSON.
  \item Generate zone-level embeddings via the trained HTE encoder (3.2M parameters).
  \item Run 5 independent Langevin chains (100 steps each, Bernoulli initialisation, temperature annealing from $\tau=1.0$ to $\tau=0.1$) to produce 5 binary candidates.
  \item For each candidate: run the feasibility decoder, then the two-stage LP worker.
  \item Select the candidate with the lowest LP objective as the pipeline's solution.
\end{enumerate}

The following metrics are computed for each scenario:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Cost gap (\%).} Relative deviation of the pipeline objective from the MILP objective: $\Delta C = (C_{\mathrm{pipe}} - C_{\mathrm{MILP}}) / |C_{\mathrm{MILP}}| \times 100$. A negative gap indicates the pipeline found a \emph{better} solution than the MILP (possible when the MILP hit its time limit).
  \item \textbf{Speedup.} Ratio of MILP solve time to pipeline total time: $S = T_{\mathrm{MILP}} / T_{\mathrm{pipe}}$, where $T_{\mathrm{pipe}}$ is the wall-clock time for the full best-of-5 pipeline (i.e.\ graph build + HTE encoding + EBM sampling + $5\times$ decoder + $5\times$ LP solve + selection). Values $>1$ indicate the pipeline is faster.
  \item \textbf{Slack (MWh).} Total unserved energy and overgeneration in the pipeline solution, a direct measure of constraint satisfaction.
  \item \textbf{LP stage used.} Which stage of the LP worker was needed (hard-fix, repair-20, repair-100, or full-soft), indicating the quality of the EBM's binary predictions.
\end{itemize}

\label{sec:main_results}

The central empirical finding of this paper can be stated as follows:

\emph{The relative computational advantage of the hybrid MILP--GNN--EBM pipeline scales positively with the combinatorial hardness of the underlying flexibility problem: Spearman rank correlation between criticality index and speedup is $\rho = +0.224$ ($p < 10^{-4}$), and between problem size and speedup is $\rho = +0.299$ ($p < 10^{-7}$).}
\end{quote}

Panel~(C) shows that the cost gap's median remains bounded even as complexity increases, with the interquartile range widening for the hardest percentiles---reflecting the EBM's partial generalisation to unseen topologies.

\subsubsection{Pipeline timing breakdown}

The neural components (graph build, HTE embedding, EBM sampling) are executed only once per scenario and contribute a roughly constant overhead of $\sim$4\,s regardless of scenario size, thanks to GPU parallelism. The feasibility decoder is negligible ($<1$\,s per sample).

This breakdown highlights an important architectural property: the pipeline's scalability bottleneck is the LP solver, not the neural network. The EBM and encoder scale with zone count but remain fast on GPU, whereas the LP solver must process the full MILP constraint matrix. Although the decoder already warm-starts the LP solver with its continuous dispatch plan (a capability whose importance is confirmed by the no-decoder ablation in Appendix~\ref{app:no_decoder}), replacing the greedy warm-start with a learned, higher-quality initial point (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}) could further reduce this bottleneck.

\subsection{Economic Advantage Analysis}
\label{sec:economic_advantage}

To formalise the speed--quality trade-off, we define an \emph{economic advantage} indicator that monetises solve time. Following the framework of Section~\ref{sec:eval_protocol}, let $\lambda$ (EUR/s) represent the operational cost of waiting (outage risk, standby cost, market opportunity cost). The total cost of a solution method is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{TotalCost}(\text{method}) = C_{\mathrm{solution}} + \lambda \cdot T_{\mathrm{solve}},
  \label{eq:total_cost}
\end{equation}
and the pipeline's advantage over MILP is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Advantage} = \mathrm{TotalCost}(\text{MILP}) - \mathrm{TotalCost}(\text{Pipeline}) = -\Delta C + \lambda \cdot \Delta T,
  \label{eq:advantage}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta C = C_{\mathrm{pipe}} - C_{\mathrm{MILP}}$ is the cost gap and $\Delta T = T_{\mathrm{MILP}} - T_{\mathrm{pipe}}$ is the time saved. A positive advantage means the pipeline is overall cheaper.

Figure~\ref{fig:lambda_heatmap} presents a heatmap of the percentage of scenarios for which the pipeline is profitable, stratified by family and $\lambda$. The high-criticality family reaches profitability for a non-trivial fraction of scenarios even at very low $\lambda$ values, because many of its scenarios exhibit large time savings ($\Delta T > 1\,000$\,s). Conversely, the low-criticality family remains unprofitable across all tested $\lambda$ values, reflecting both the absence of time savings and the positive cost gap. The medium family occupies an intermediate position.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig5_lambda_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Percentage of scenarios where the pipeline is economically profitable (positive advantage), as a function of $\lambda$ (EUR/s) and criticality family. Green cells indicate high profitability; red cells indicate low profitability.}
  \label{fig:lambda_heatmap}
\end{figure}

The heatmap reveals that economic profitability is dominated by the \emph{cost gap term} rather than the time-value term: even at $\lambda = 100$\,EUR/s, the overall profitability remains at $\sim$29\%, because the cost gap on low- and medium-criticality scenarios overwhelms the time savings. This motivates a \emph{selective deployment} strategy: rather than applying the pipeline uniformly, it should be targeted at the scenarios where it provides the greatest value---those with high criticality and long expected MILP solve times.

The high hard-fix rate (73\%) on this subset is noteworthy: it indicates that the EBM produces binary predictions of sufficient quality that the LP worker can resolve them in a single pass, even for the largest and hardest instances.

\paragraph{Contrast with optimally-solved scenarios.}
The 59 scenarios solved optimally by the MILP exhibit a strikingly different pattern (Table~\ref{tab:htl_comparison}): the pipeline's median cost gap is $-112$\%, meaning it finds solutions whose reported objective is roughly half the MILP's optimal cost. These strongly negative gaps are largely an artefact of the full-soft LP relaxation: since only 17\% of these scenarios reach Stage~1 (hard-fix), the majority proceed to the full-soft stage where \emph{all} binary variables are relaxed to continuous. The resulting LP solution exploits fractional commitment decisions---e.g., a unit can be ``half on,'' avoiding startup costs while still contributing partial output---producing artificially low objectives that no integer-feasible dispatch could achieve. In contrast, the MILP's optimal solution respects all integrality constraints, so the comparison is between a relaxed LP bound and a true MILP optimum. This explains why the gap is systematically negative on optimally-solved instances: it reflects the integrality gap of the underlying formulation rather than genuine solution superiority. This duality---strong on time-limited, misleadingly negative on optimally-solved---reinforces the case for criticality-aware routing and caution when interpreting aggregate cost gap statistics.


\subsection{Implications for Large-Scale Flexibility Exploration}
\label{sec:implications}

The experimental results have direct implications for operational planning workflows that require solving hundreds to thousands of what-if scenarios:

\paragraph{Criticality-aware solver routing.}
The sharp dependence of pipeline performance on scenario criticality suggests a natural \emph{hybrid strategy}: use the MILP solver for low-criticality scenarios (where it is fast and exact) and the pipeline for high-criticality ones (where it is faster and equally good). The criticality index (Section~\ref{sec:criticality}), which is computable in milliseconds from the scenario parameters alone, provides a natural routing criterion. A threshold of $C \approx 0.40$ would capture the transition point where the pipeline's speedup exceeds $1\times$.

\paragraph{Throughput gains at portfolio scale.}
Consider a planning exercise requiring 1\,000 scenarios with a realistic criticality distribution (e.g., 40\% low, 30\% medium, 30\% high). Under the hybrid strategy, the low scenarios are solved by the MILP in $\sim$400\,s total, the medium ones in $\sim$4\,000\,s (MILP) or $\sim$8\,000\,s (pipeline), and the high ones in $\sim$25\,000\,s (MILP, with many time-outs) or $\sim$24\,000\,s (pipeline, all feasible). The key advantage is not raw throughput but \emph{predictability}: the pipeline's time per scenario is bounded and does not explode for hard instances, enabling reliable batch scheduling.

\paragraph{Solution diversity.}
By running multiple Langevin chains (5 in our experiments), the pipeline produces a \emph{pool} of diverse feasible solutions per scenario. The best-of-5 selection provides a natural hedge against local minima. In a planning context, the diversity of near-optimal solutions is itself valuable: it reveals which operational decisions are robust (consistent across candidates) and which are sensitive to minor perturbations.

\paragraph{Honest assessment of current limitations.}
The pipeline's median cost gap of 14.4\% across all families indicates that it does not yet match MILP quality on the majority of scenarios. The main bottlenecks are: (i) the EBM's generalisation to large topologies unseen during training, reflected in the 53\% full-soft rate for high-criticality scenarios; (ii) the feasibility decoder's greedy approximation, which introduces sub-optimalities that the LP worker cannot fully recover; and (iii) the fixed pipeline overhead ($\sim$4\,s), which dominates for easy scenarios. These limitations are addressable through larger and more diverse training sets, a learned (rather than greedy) decoder, and batched GPU inference, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\subsection{Persona-Based Scenario Evaluation}
\label{sec:persona_scenarios}

The preceding analysis used three evaluation families that differ along a single axis---the overall criticality level. In practice, however, different stakeholders stress different aspects of the power system. A VRE developer cares about storage adequacy under high renewable penetration; a network operator focuses on congestion and cross-border flows; an operations researcher may target pure combinatorial hardness. To assess how the pipeline responds to these heterogeneous stress profiles, we introduce \emph{persona-based} scenario families that weight the criticality drivers differently. Persona-based scenarios thus approximate the perspectives of key stakeholders in modern power systems. Evaluating optimisation pipelines under these differentiated stress regimes helps determine whether hybrid optimisation–learning approaches remain robust across the operational challenges faced by real electricity systems.

The mean speedup of $2.6\times$ masks a bimodal distribution: very fast on time-limited instances but slower than the MILP on easy ones.

\subsubsection{Implications}

The persona evaluation demonstrates that the pipeline's performance profile is not captured by a single criticality score. Two scenarios with identical overall criticality can elicit very different pipeline behaviour depending on \emph{which} drivers are stressed:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Stress-dominated scenarios} (VRE/Battery) are typically small enough for the MILP to solve quickly, so the pipeline's advantage is quality and feasibility, not speed.
  \item \textbf{Network-dominated scenarios} (Network Operator) produce the largest MILP time-outs and the clearest speedup advantage, confirming that cross-border congestion and large topologies are the pipeline's sweet spot.
  \item \textbf{Hardness-dominated scenarios} (Mathematician) exploit the EBM's generalisation weakness on dense combinatorial structure, pushing most scenarios to full-soft repair, but still yield superior objectives because the MILP's own incumbents are weak.
\end{itemize}

These findings motivate persona-aware solver routing: rather than a single criticality threshold, a routing policy should consider the \emph{type} of difficulty (stress vs.\ hardness) to decide when to deploy the pipeline vs.\ the MILP.

\subsection{Out-of-Distribution Assessment: Extreme-Criticality Scenarios}
\label{sec:ood_extreme}

The preceding evaluation (Sections~\ref{sec:pareto}--\ref{sec:persona_scenarios}) used three evaluation families that differ by overall criticality level, plus three persona-based families that stress different driver axes. This subsection assesses the pipeline's robustness on a deliberately \emph{out-of-distribution} (OOD) test set of 100 extreme-criticality scenarios designed to stress the MILP solver beyond its operational limits.

\subsubsection{Scenario characteristics}

The extreme scenarios are generated by maximising the criticality drivers that correlate most strongly with MILP solve time. Compared to the high-criticality evaluation family, the extreme set amplifies four primary axes: SOC tightness (B8, mean $\tilde{x}_{B8} = 0.96$), inverse storage energy adequacy (A8, mean $\tilde{x}_{A8} = 0.94$), number of binary variables (B3, $+0.45$ delta vs.\ high family), and number of zones (B1). The elevated cost gap motivates two targeted improvements: (i)~augmenting the training set with extreme-criticality instances (addressing limitation \textbf{L1}), and (ii)~improving the decoder's binary modifications, which are shown to degrade EBM decisions on these instances (see Appendix~\ref{app:no_decoder}).

\section{Conclusions and Research Avenues}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary}

This paper introduced a hybrid \textbf{MILP--GNN--EBM} methodology for near-real-time exploration of multi-scale power-system flexibility scenarios. The framework comprises six tightly integrated components: (i)~a synthetic scenario generator with diversity maximisation and ex-ante criticality indexing (Section~\ref{sec:scenario_generator}); (ii)~a multi-layer MILP oracle that coordinates thermal commitment, storage scheduling, demand response, and cross-border exchanges under network and inter-temporal constraints (Section~\ref{sec:milp_oracle}); (iii)~a projection of each scenario onto a heterogeneous temporal graph that mirrors the physical containment hierarchy (Section~\ref{sec:graph_projection}); (iv)~a Hierarchical Temporal Encoder (HTE) trained via self-supervised contrastive learning to produce zone-level embeddings without MILP labels (Section~\ref{sec:hte}); (v)~a conditional graph Energy-Based Model (EBM) with Langevin sampling that learns an energy landscape over binary commitment decisions and generates diverse candidates in parallel (Section~\ref{sec:ebm}); and (vi)~a feasibility decoder and two-stage LP worker that repair binary predictions into physically consistent, economically optimised dispatch plans (Section~\ref{sec:reconstruction}).

The central research question asked whether such a hybrid methodology can enable near-real-time exploration of flexibility scenarios---and remain effective as scenarios become increasingly complex. The experimental evaluation on 300 out-of-sample scenarios across three criticality families (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}), complemented by a systematic discussion of MILP dominance regimes and failure modes (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) and a persona-based evaluation exploring heterogeneous stakeholder stress profiles (Section~\ref{sec:persona_scenarios}), provides a nuanced answer.


\subsection{Key Findings}

\paragraph{The pipeline's value scales with scenario difficulty.}
The strongest result is the clear positive correlation between scenario criticality and pipeline advantage. On high-criticality scenarios---large topologies (80--160 zones), stressed demand, many binary variables---the pipeline achieves a median cost gap of $-0.04$\% with a median speedup of $0.88\times$ and a mean speedup of $5.1\times$. For the 41 scenarios where the MILP hit its 20-minute time limit, the pipeline delivers a median speedup of $11.5\times$ (range $[3.3\times, 21.4\times]$) with 100\% of instances solved faster. This confirms the core design hypothesis: learned heuristics are most valuable precisely where exact solvers are most expensive.

\paragraph{Feasibility is guaranteed, not merely likely.}
Across all 300 evaluation scenarios, the pipeline achieves a 100\% feasibility rate with near-zero slack ($<0.01$\,MWh). This is not a statistical claim but a structural guarantee: the three-phase greedy decoder enforces power balance at every zone and timestep, and the LP worker's progressive repair eliminates residual violations. This property is essential for any deployment in operational planning, where infeasible solutions are categorically rejected.

\paragraph{The EBM learns meaningful binary patterns.}
The LP worker's stage distribution provides a direct measure of the EBM's prediction quality. The overall hard-fix rate of 65\%---meaning that for two-thirds of scenarios, the EBM's binary decisions are directly usable without any repair---demonstrates that the two-step training procedure (contrastive divergence pre-training followed by LP-guided preference fine-tuning) successfully captures the MILP's implicit commitment logic. The 90\% hard-fix rate on low-criticality scenarios confirms strong learning on the well-represented part of the training distribution.

\paragraph{Honest cost gap assessment.}
The pipeline does not yet match MILP quality across the board: the overall median cost gap of 14.4\% reflects the approximation cost of the greedy decoder and the EBM's limited generalisation to large, unseen topologies. On low-criticality scenarios, where the MILP solves in under 2 seconds, there is no practical need for the pipeline and the cost gap is a pure overhead. The pipeline's current sweet spot is the regime where MILP solve times exceed 1--2 minutes, and its value increases monotonically with problem complexity.


\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

We identify seven limitations of the current framework, organised into methodological (\textbf{L1--L4}), statistical (\textbf{L5}), and validation (\textbf{L6--L7}) categories. Each limitation suggests a concrete research direction (Section~\ref{sec:research_avenues}).

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{L\arabic*}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{EBM generalisation to large topologies.}
  The training set of 5\,000 scenarios contains relatively few instances with $>100$ zones, resulting in a 53\% full-soft rate on high-criticality scenarios (vs.\ 10\% on low-criticality). The EBM's zone-level GRU architecture processes each zone independently before aggregation, limiting its ability to capture inter-zonal coordination patterns at scales far beyond those seen during training. The correlation analysis (Table~\ref{tab:correlations}) confirms that the cost gap is \emph{not} structurally correlated with problem size ($\rho = -0.03$, $p = 0.58$), suggesting that the failures are distributional (insufficient training coverage) rather than architectural.

  \item \textbf{Greedy decoder sub-optimality.}
  The feasibility decoder is a rule-based, merit-order heuristic that processes one timestep at a time. It cannot look ahead (e.g., to anticipate an evening peak when deciding whether to charge a battery at noon) and may introduce dispatch sub-optimalities that the LP worker inherits as fixed binary decisions. The 12.6\% median gap on low-criticality scenarios---where 90\% of EBM predictions are directly usable---is largely attributable to the decoder rather than the EBM.

  \item \textbf{Fixed pipeline overhead.}
  The neural components (graph construction, HTE embedding, $5\times$ Langevin sampling) contribute $\sim$4\,s of overhead per scenario regardless of problem size. For the simplest scenarios (MILP $<2$\,s), this overhead alone makes the pipeline slower. The MILP dominance analysis (Table~\ref{tab:milp_dominance}) quantifies this: for MILP times $<2$\,s, the pipeline yields a better solution in only 5\% of cases.

  \item \textbf{Single-horizon, deterministic setting.}
  The current MILP oracle and the entire pipeline operate on a single 24-hour deterministic horizon. Real-world flexibility planning requires multi-day or multi-week rolling horizons, stochastic scenarios with correlated forecast errors, and security constraints (N$-$1 contingencies) that substantially increase the binary decision space.

  \item \textbf{Training distribution dependency.}
  The pipeline's performance is fundamentally conditioned on the training distribution. The EBM and HTE encoder have only seen scenarios generated by our synthetic scenario generator; there is no guarantee that the learned energy landscape generalises to scenarios produced by a different generator or drawn from historical operational data. The Mann-Whitney tests (Table~\ref{tab:stat_tests}) show that cost gap distributions do not differ significantly between families ($p > 0.05$), but this may reflect the shared generative process rather than true robustness.

  \item \textbf{LP worker as the scalability bottleneck.}
  The pipeline timing breakdown (Figure~\ref{fig:timing_breakdown}) reveals that the LP solve accounts for $>90$\% of total time on high-criticality scenarios. Since the LP worker solves the full MILP constraint matrix with binary variables fixed, its time scales with the number of continuous variables and constraints---i.e., with problem size. Without warm-starting or LP decomposition, the pipeline's scalability to systems with $>200$ zones will be limited by the LP solver, not by the neural components.

  \item \textbf{No validation on real-world operational data.}
  All 5\,300 scenarios (5\,000 training + 300 evaluation) are synthetically generated. While the scenario generator covers a broad range of realistic techno-economic parameters (Section~\ref{sec:scenario_generator}), it does not reproduce the spatial and temporal correlations present in actual grid data (e.g., correlated equipment outages, spatially coherent weather patterns, market-driven strategic behaviour). Validation on data from a real transmission system operator is necessary to establish the pipeline's practical applicability, but such data is rarely publicly available at the required granularity.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Research Avenues}
\label{sec:research_avenues}

The limitations above, together with the empirical insights from Section~\ref{sec:experiments}, point to several concrete directions for future work.

\paragraph{R1: Scaling the EBM to larger topologies.}
Two complementary strategies can address \textbf{L1}: (i)~augmenting the training set with more large-topology scenarios, potentially via curriculum sampling that over-represents the high-criticality tail; and (ii)~replacing the independent zone-level GRU with a spatial attention mechanism (e.g., a graph Transformer over the zone-level graph at each timestep) that explicitly models inter-zonal coordination. The latter would allow the EBM to capture cross-zone correlations---such as thermal commitment patterns driven by transmission congestion---that the current architecture misses at large scales.

\paragraph{R2: Learned feasibility decoder.}
The greedy decoder (\textbf{L2}) could be replaced or augmented by a learned component---for example, a recurrent policy that takes the EBM's binary suggestions, the scenario physics, and the current storage states as input and outputs a full dispatch plan with lookahead. Training such a decoder end-to-end through the LP worker's cost signal (analogous to the EBM's preference fine-tuning) would allow it to anticipate the LP worker's corrections and reduce the cost gap. Differentiable constraint layers~\parencite{zeng_glinsat_2024} could further enforce hard constraints within the learned decoder.

\paragraph{R3: Stochastic and multi-period extensions.}
Addressing \textbf{L4} is the most ambitious direction. In a stochastic setting, the scenario generator would produce \emph{scenario trees} rather than single trajectories, and the MILP oracle would become a two-stage or multi-stage stochastic program. The EBM could be conditioned on a distribution over embeddings (rather than a single embedding) to capture uncertainty, and the Langevin sampler could explore the joint space of here-and-now and wait-and-see binary decisions. Rolling-horizon deployment would further require the encoder and EBM to process overlapping time windows with state carry-over.

\paragraph{R4: Batched and streaming inference.}
Addressing \textbf{L3} requires a re-engineering of the inference pipeline for batch GPU processing. The graph construction and HTE encoding steps already support batched operation via PyTorch Geometric's data loader; extending this to batched Langevin sampling and parallelised LP solving (e.g., via multi-threaded HiGHS instances) could reduce the per-scenario amortised cost to under 1\,s for small scenarios, making the pipeline competitive even in the low-criticality regime.

\paragraph{R5: Criticality-aware hybrid deployment.}
The experimental results strongly motivate a production architecture where the criticality index (computable in milliseconds) routes each incoming scenario to either the MILP solver or the pipeline. Formalising this routing as an online learning problem---where the threshold adapts to observed pipeline performance and changing scenario distributions---would make the hybrid strategy self-tuning and robust to distribution shift.

\paragraph{R6: Security constraints and N$-$1 contingencies.}
Operational planning increasingly requires that dispatch solutions remain feasible under single-element outages (N$-$1 security). Integrating contingency constraints into the MILP oracle would enrich the training labels and allow the EBM to learn commitment patterns that are robust to equipment failure. The criticality index could be extended with a security dimension (e.g., contingency severity score) to guide the hybrid solver-routing strategy.


\paragraph{R7: Transfer across system configurations.}
The current pipeline is trained and evaluated on a single synthetic scenario generator. A natural extension is to test whether the HTE embeddings and EBM energy function transfer to scenarios generated from different system configurations (e.g., different countries, different asset mixes, different market designs) with minimal or no fine-tuning. The self-supervised nature of the HTE training and the graph-based representation provide a favourable inductive bias for such transfer, but empirical validation is needed.


\subsection{Closing Remarks}

The hybrid MILP--GNN--EBM methodology demonstrates that combining the rigour of mathematical programming with the speed of learned inference is a viable path toward interactive scenario exploration in power-system flexibility studies. The pipeline does not replace the MILP solver---it extends its reach to regimes where repeated exact solves are impractical. By design, every solution the pipeline produces is feasible, economically meaningful, and accompanied by a cost gap estimate relative to the MILP oracle, enabling operators to make informed decisions about when to trust the fast approximation and when to invest in an exact solve.

The research avenues outlined above---from architectural improvements (R1--R3) to deployment engineering (R4--R5) and methodological extensions (R6--R7)---trace a path from the current proof-of-concept toward a production-grade exploration engine for the multi-scale flexibility challenges that characterise the energy transition.

\section{Criticality index: metric definitions}
\label{app:criticality}

\subsection{Normalization and aggregation}
Each raw metric $x_k(s)$ is mapped to $[0,1]$ through bounded min--max scaling:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x}_k(s)=\mathrm{clip}\!\left(\frac{x_k(s)-\ell_k}{u_k-\ell_k},\,0,\,1\right),
\label{eq:crit_norm}
\end{equation}
where $(\ell_k,u_k)$ are metric-specific bounds (static, or data-driven percentile bounds).
The stress and hardness indices are computed as weighted sums of normalized metrics:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Stress}(s)=\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}_S} w_k^{(S)}\,\tilde{x}_k(s),\qquad
\mathrm{Hard}(s)=\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}_H} w_k^{(H)}\,\tilde{x}_k(s),
\end{equation}
and the overall criticality index is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Crit}(s)=\alpha\,\mathrm{Stress}(s)+(1-\alpha)\,\mathrm{Hard}(s),\qquad \alpha\in[0,1].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Metric definitions}
\label{app:crit_metrics}

We list below the 22 raw metrics used in the implementation. For readability we define the common proxy constants used in the code and reuse them across metrics.

\paragraph{Common proxy constants}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Average unit sizes: $\overline{P}_{\mathrm{th}}=300$ MW (thermal), $\overline{P}_{\mathrm{nuc}}=1000$ MW (nuclear).
\item Average zonal demand: $\overline{D}_Z=500$ MW.
\item Peak multiplier: $\kappa_{\mathrm{peak}}=1.3$.
\item Capacity factor proxy for variable-cost scaling: $\kappa=0.5$.
\item Variable cost proxy: $c_{\mathrm{var}}=60$ EUR/MWh.
\item Startup cost proxies: $c^{\mathrm{SU}}_{\mathrm{th}}=5000$ EUR, $c^{\mathrm{SU}}_{\mathrm{nuc}}=30000$ EUR.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Auxiliary quantities.}
Let $n_Z$ be the number of zones, $n_{\mathrm{th}}$ and $n_{\mathrm{nuc}}$ the number of thermal and nuclear units, $H$ the horizon in hours, and $\lambda_D$ the demand scale factor.
We use the firm-capacity and peak-demand proxies:
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{firm}}(s)=n_{\mathrm{th}}\overline{P}_{\mathrm{th}}+n_{\mathrm{nuc}}\overline{P}_{\mathrm{nuc}},
\qquad
D_{\mathrm{peak}}(s)=n_Z\,\overline{D}_Z\,\lambda_D\,\kappa_{\mathrm{peak}}.
\label{eq:aux_peak_firm}
\end{equation}
We also define a residual-peak proxy (used in A7--A9):
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{res,peak}}(s)=D_{\mathrm{peak}}(s)\Bigl(1-0.3\,x_{A1}(s)\Bigr).
\label{eq:aux_respeak}
\end{equation}
The clipping operator used in several metrics is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{clip}(y,a,b)=\max\{a,\min\{y,b\}\}.
\label{eq:clip}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{A. Physical stress metrics (A1--A12)}

\paragraph{A1: VRE penetration ratio.}
Definition:
\begin{equation}
x_{A1}(s)=\frac{p^{\mathrm{VRE}}}{100}.
\end{equation}
Here $p^{\mathrm{VRE}}$ is the VRE penetration in percent.

\paragraph{}High VRE penetration displaces dispatchable generation and introduces forecast-dependent supply variability. Criticality emerges because the residual demand (demand minus VRE infeed) becomes more volatile and less predictable, forcing the unit-commitment solver to explore more binary on/off combinations for thermal and storage assets to maintain power balance across all timesteps.

\paragraph{A2: Residual demand volatility (proxy).}
Definition:
\begin{equation}
x_{A2}(s)=\sigma_{\mathrm{res}},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\mathrm{res}}$ is a scenario-level proxy of net/residual demand volatility.

\paragraph{}Residual demand volatility measures how much the net load (demand minus must-run renewables) fluctuates over the horizon. High volatility requires frequent changes in thermal commitment and storage mode, multiplying the number of binary switching decisions the solver must evaluate. It is also a primary driver of ramping stress: fast swings in residual demand push units toward their ramp limits and increase the risk of infeasibility.

\paragraph{A3: Peak-to-valley ratio (proxy).}
Definition:
\begin{equation}
x_{A3}(s)=\mathrm{PTV},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{PTV}$ is a scenario-level proxy of the peak-to-valley ratio of net demand.

\paragraph{}A large peak-to-valley ratio means the system must cycle between low-load valleys (where units are decommitted to avoid minimum-generation violations) and high-load peaks (where all available capacity is needed). This amplifies the number of startup/shutdown transitions, each of which is a binary decision with associated costs. The wider the ratio, the more the solver must trade off startup costs against operating costs across the horizon.

\paragraph{A4: Short-term variability (weather-profile mapping).}
Let $\omega$ be a categorical weather label. The implementation maps $\omega$ to a scalar:
\begin{equation}
x_{A4}(s)=
\begin{cases}
0.08 & \omega=\texttt{calm\_winter},\\
0.22 & \omega=\texttt{stormy\_winter},\\
0.18 & \omega=\texttt{sunny\_summer},\\
0.06 & \omega=\texttt{overcast\_summer},\\
0.14 & \omega=\texttt{mixed},\\
0.12 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Weather profiles encode intra-day renewable variability patterns that are not captured by the aggregate VRE penetration ratio. Stormy winter conditions produce rapid wind ramps and cloud transients that create short-horizon supply shocks; sunny summer conditions cause steep solar ramps at sunrise and sunset. Criticality emerges from the mismatch between the timescale of these weather-driven fluctuations and the response time of dispatchable units (constrained by ramp limits and minimum up/down times).

\paragraph{A5: Demand scale factor.}
Definition:
\begin{equation}
x_{A5}(s)=\lambda_D.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}The demand scale factor uniformly amplifies all zonal loads, pushing the system closer to its generation capacity limits. Criticality emerges because higher demand tightens the supply--demand balance, reduces available reserves, and forces the solver to commit marginal units whose on/off status has a significant impact on both cost and feasibility. At high $\lambda_D$, even small perturbations in renewable output can trigger unserved energy or costly emergency actions.

\paragraph{A6: Peak-to-firm capacity ratio (proxy).}
Using \eqref{eq:aux_peak_firm}, define:
\begin{equation}
x_{A6}(s)=\frac{D_{\mathrm{peak}}(s)}{\max\!\left(P_{\mathrm{firm}}(s),1\right)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}This ratio captures the system's \emph{adequacy margin}: when peak demand approaches or exceeds firm (dispatchable) capacity, the system has no room for error. Criticality emerges because a ratio near or above~1 means that every dispatchable unit must be committed at peak, leaving no reserves. The solver must then find the exact commitment schedule that threads the needle between adequacy and cost minimisation, with binary decisions becoming highly consequential.

\paragraph{A7: Inverse storage power adequacy (proxy).}
Let $P^{\mathrm{stor}}$ be total storage power (MW). Using \eqref{eq:aux_respeak}:
\begin{equation}
x_{A7}(s)=\mathrm{clip}\!\left(1-\frac{P^{\mathrm{stor}}}{\max\!\left(D_{\mathrm{res,peak}}(s),1\right)},-1,1\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Storage power adequacy measures whether the installed storage fleet can inject enough power to cover residual-demand peaks. The metric is inverted (higher = less adequate) so that it increases with stress. Criticality emerges because insufficient storage power forces the solver to rely on slower, costlier flexibility resources (thermal ramping, demand response, cross-border imports) and to optimise the precise charge/discharge timing---a tightly coupled binary-and-continuous decision across the full horizon.

\paragraph{A8: Inverse storage energy adequacy (proxy).}
Let $E^{\mathrm{stor}}$ be total storage energy (MWh). Define a proxy for cumulative positive residual energy:
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{pos}}(s)=0.4\,H\,D_{\mathrm{res,peak}}(s),
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
x_{A8}(s)=\mathrm{clip}\!\left(1-\frac{E^{\mathrm{stor}}}{\max\!\left(S_{\mathrm{pos}}(s),1\right)},-1,1\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}While A7 captures instantaneous power adequacy, A8 captures \emph{energy} adequacy: whether the storage fleet can absorb and release enough cumulative energy over the horizon. Criticality emerges because limited energy capacity forces the solver to optimise the temporal allocation of storage cycles precisely---charging too early or too late can leave the system without reserves when they are most needed. This creates strong inter-temporal coupling between storage SOC variables across all timesteps.

\paragraph{A9: Inverse thermal flexibility (proxy).}
Let $\rho_{\mathrm{ramp}}$ be the thermal ramp percentage parameter. Define the thermal flexibility proxy:
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{th,flex}}(s)=n_{\mathrm{th}}\,\overline{P}_{\mathrm{th}}\,\rho_{\mathrm{ramp}},
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}
x_{A9}(s)=\mathrm{clip}\!\left(1-\frac{P_{\mathrm{th,flex}}(s)}{\max\!\left(D_{\mathrm{res,peak}}(s),1\right)},-1,1\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Thermal flexibility is the primary means of following residual-demand variations in conventional systems. The inverse metric increases when the fleet's aggregate ramping capability is small relative to the residual-demand peak. Criticality emerges because low thermal flexibility means that the solver cannot simply ramp existing committed units up or down; instead, it must commit or decommit additional units (binary decisions) to track demand, incurring startup costs and minimum-up/down-time constraints that tighten the feasible space.

\paragraph{A10: Inverse DR headroom (proxy).}
Let $P^{\mathrm{DR}}$ be total demand-response capacity (MW). Define a proxy for total energy demand over the horizon:
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{tot}}(s)=n_Z\,\overline{D}_Z\,\lambda_D\,H,
\end{equation}
and a DR headroom proxy
\begin{equation}
h_{\mathrm{DR}}(s)=\frac{P^{\mathrm{DR}}H}{\max\!\left(D_{\mathrm{tot}}(s),1\right)}.
\end{equation}
Then the implemented metric is
\begin{equation}
x_{A10}(s)=\mathrm{clip}\!\left(1-h_{\mathrm{DR}}(s),0,1\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Demand response provides a fast, binary flexibility resource (shed or not shed) that can substitute for costly generation commitment changes. When DR headroom is low (high $x_{A10}$), this flexibility cushion is unavailable, and the solver must rely entirely on supply-side adjustments. Criticality emerges because the loss of DR headroom eliminates a degree of freedom that would otherwise help the solver satisfy power-balance constraints during stress periods without committing additional thermal units.

\paragraph{A11: Trade reliance (cross-border policy proxy).}
Let $\pi_{\mathrm{xb}}\in\{\texttt{allow},\texttt{cap},\texttt{block}\}$ be the cross-border policy label and $n_{\mathrm{nb}}$ the number of neighbouring nations.
The code defines
\begin{equation}
\phi(\pi_{\mathrm{xb}})=
\begin{cases}
0.25 & \pi_{\mathrm{xb}}=\texttt{allow},\\
0.15 & \pi_{\mathrm{xb}}=\texttt{cap},\\
0.02 & \pi_{\mathrm{xb}}=\texttt{block},\\
0.15 & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
and sets
\begin{equation}
x_{A11}(s)=\phi(\pi_{\mathrm{xb}})\cdot \min\!\left(\frac{n_{\mathrm{nb}}}{4},1\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Trade reliance captures the extent to which the system depends on cross-border exchanges to balance supply and demand. High trade reliance (permissive cross-border policy with many neighbours) introduces import/export flow variables and associated binary mode decisions (import vs.\ export per zone per timestep), expanding the MILP's decision space. Criticality emerges because the solver must jointly optimise internal dispatch and cross-border flows, with the flow directions introducing additional combinatorial choices that couple zones spatially.

\paragraph{A12: Inverse congestion exposure (intertie density proxy).}
Let $\rho_{\mathrm{it}}$ be the intertie density parameter. The implemented proxy is
\begin{equation}
x_{A12}(s)=1-\rho_{\mathrm{it}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Intertie density measures the proportion of possible inter-regional transmission links that are actually built. The inverse metric increases when the network is sparse. Criticality emerges because a sparse network limits the system's ability to redistribute power across regions: local surpluses cannot be exported and local deficits cannot be relieved by imports, forcing each region to solve its own commitment problem almost independently. This creates congestion bottlenecks that tighten the feasible space and can push the solver toward expensive local solutions or unserved energy.

% B) Combinatorial hardness metrics (B1--B10)
% ============================================================

\subsubsection*{B. Combinatorial hardness metrics (B1--B10)}

\paragraph{B1: Number of zones.}
Definition:
\begin{equation}
x_{B1}(s)=n_Z.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}The number of zones is the most direct measure of problem scale. Each additional zone introduces its own set of generation, storage, and demand-response binary variables, its own power-balance constraint, and coupling constraints with neighbouring zones via transmission links. Criticality emerges because MILP solve time grows super-linearly with the number of zones: more zones mean more binary variables, more constraints, and a larger branch-and-bound tree.

\paragraph{B2: Horizon length in number of timesteps.}
Let $\Delta t$ be the time step length in minutes. The number of timesteps is
\begin{equation}
n_T=\left\lfloor \frac{60H}{\Delta t}\right\rfloor,
\end{equation}
and the implemented metric is
\begin{equation}
x_{B2}(s)=n_T.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}The horizon length multiplies the problem size along the temporal axis: each additional timestep replicates the full set of per-zone variables and constraints. Criticality emerges because longer horizons also strengthen inter-temporal coupling (storage SOC continuity, minimum up/down times, ramp limits carry state from one timestep to the next), making the LP relaxation weaker and branch-and-bound more expensive.

\paragraph{B3: Estimated number of binary variables.}
Let $\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{bin}}$ be read from \texttt{diff.n\_binary\_variables}; if missing, the code uses the fallback
$\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{bin}}=0.1\times\texttt{estimates.vars\_total}$. Then
\begin{equation}
x_{B3}(s)=\widehat{n}_{\mathrm{bin}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}The total number of binary variables is the single most predictive feature of MILP solve time in unit-commitment problems \parencite{morales_espana_tight_2013}. Each binary variable doubles the size of the feasible space that branch-and-bound must explore in the worst case. Criticality emerges directly: more binaries mean a combinatorially larger search tree and, empirically, longer solve times that often exceed solver time limits.

\paragraph{B4: Thermal unit density.}
Definition:
\begin{equation}
x_{B4}(s)=\frac{n_{\mathrm{th}}}{\max(n_Z,1)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Thermal unit density measures the number of individually committed thermal generators per zone. Unlike B3, which counts total binaries, B4 captures the \emph{density} of commitment decisions within each zone's sub-problem. Criticality emerges because dense thermal fleets create many near-equivalent commitment combinations (e.g., committing unit~A vs.\ unit~B at similar costs), producing a flat objective landscape that slows branch-and-bound convergence. This is the classic symmetry problem in unit-commitment MILP formulations.

\paragraph{B5: Minimum-generation tightness (constant proxy).}
The current implementation uses a constant proxy:
\begin{equation}
x_{B5}(s)=0.35.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Minimum-generation tightness measures the ratio $\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}} / \overline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}$ of the thermal fleet. A high ratio means committed units cannot reduce output below a significant fraction of their capacity, creating \emph{must-run} blocks that constrain the power balance. Criticality emerges because tight minimum-generation bounds force the solver to decommit units entirely (a binary decision) rather than simply reducing their output, increasing the number of on/off transitions. The current implementation uses a constant proxy pending per-unit data.

\paragraph{B6: Startup cost intensity (proxy).}
Define the total startup cost proxy
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{SU}}(s)=n_{\mathrm{th}}c^{\mathrm{SU}}_{\mathrm{th}}+n_{\mathrm{nuc}}c^{\mathrm{SU}}_{\mathrm{nuc}},
\end{equation}
and the variable-cost proxy over the horizon
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{var}}(s)=\left(n_{\mathrm{th}}\overline{P}_{\mathrm{th}}+n_{\mathrm{nuc}}\overline{P}_{\mathrm{nuc}}\right)\cdot H \cdot \kappa \cdot c_{\mathrm{var}}.
\end{equation}
Then the metric is
\begin{equation}
x_{B6}(s)=\min\!\left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{SU}}(s)}{\max(C_{\mathrm{var}}(s),1)},\,0.5\right).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Startup cost intensity measures the ratio of fixed commitment costs (startup) to variable running costs. When startup costs are high relative to fuel costs, the on/off decision for each unit becomes economically consequential: committing a unit incurs a large sunk cost, but not committing it risks expensive alternatives (imports, DR, unserved energy). Criticality emerges because high startup intensity creates a \emph{non-convex} cost landscape where the LP relaxation significantly underestimates the true MILP cost, weakening the relaxation bound and slowing branch-and-bound convergence.

\paragraph{B7: Ramping tightness (proxy).}
Let $P_{\mathrm{th}}(s)=n_{\mathrm{th}}\overline{P}_{\mathrm{th}}$ be total thermal capacity (proxy) and
$R_{\mathrm{th}}(s)=P_{\mathrm{th}}(s)\rho_{\mathrm{ramp}}$ the corresponding ramping capability proxy.
The implemented ratio is
\begin{equation}
x_{B7}(s)=\frac{P_{\mathrm{th}}(s)}{\max(R_{\mathrm{th}}(s),1)}.
\end{equation}
(With these proxies, this simplifies to approximately $1/\rho_{\mathrm{ramp}}$ when $R_{\mathrm{th}}(s)\ge 1$.)

\paragraph{}Ramping tightness captures how constrained the thermal fleet is in adjusting output between consecutive timesteps. A high ratio (capacity $\gg$ ramp capability) means that units can only change output slowly, creating strong temporal coupling between commitment decisions: the solver must plan ahead to ensure that enough capacity is online \emph{before} demand rises. Criticality emerges because tight ramp limits interact with startup constraints to create multi-period commitment dependencies that the LP relaxation cannot capture, widening the integrality gap.

\paragraph{B8: Storage SOC tightness proxy.}
Let $h_{E/P}$ be the battery energy-to-power duration (hours) and $\tau_{\mathrm{SOC}}$ the final SOC tolerance. Then
\begin{equation}
x_{B8}(s)=\frac{h_{E/P}}{\max(\tau_{\mathrm{SOC}},0.01)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}SOC tightness measures the ratio of the battery's energy-to-power duration to the allowed SOC deviation at the end of the horizon. A high ratio means the storage system has deep energy reservoirs relative to its SOC tolerance, creating long-range temporal dependencies: the solver must plan charge/discharge cycles over many timesteps to ensure the final SOC constraint is met. Criticality emerges because the SOC continuity equation links every timestep's charge/discharge binary decision to the cumulative energy trajectory, making greedy or myopic strategies infeasible and forcing global optimisation across the full horizon.

\paragraph{B9: Interconnection density (derived proxy).}
Let $n_R$ be the number of regions and $\rho_{\mathrm{it}}$ the intertie density parameter. The code estimates the number of inter-regional links as
\begin{equation}
\widehat{n}_L(s)=\rho_{\mathrm{it}}\frac{n_R(n_R-1)}{2},
\end{equation}
and defines
\begin{equation}
x_{B9}(s)=\frac{\widehat{n}_L(s)}{\max(n_Z,1)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Interconnection density measures the number of inter-regional transmission links relative to the number of zones. Dense interconnection introduces more flow variables and transmission constraints (capacity limits, loss factors), expanding the LP's constraint matrix. Criticality emerges because each transmission link couples the power-balance constraints of two zones, creating a network of spatial dependencies. Dense networks prevent the MILP from decomposing into independent zonal sub-problems, forcing the solver to consider the full spatial coupling simultaneously.

\paragraph{B10: Network heterogeneity (zones-per-region dispersion).}
Let $\{Z_r\}_{r=1}^{n_R}$ be the number of zones in each region. The implemented heterogeneity proxy is
\begin{equation}
x_{B10}(s)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{\mathrm{std}(Z_r)}{\max(\mathrm{mean}(Z_r),1)} & n_R>1,\\[4pt]
0 & n_R=1.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{}Network heterogeneity measures the dispersion (coefficient of variation) of zone counts across regions. A heterogeneous network contains both small regions (few zones, easy sub-problems) and large regions (many zones, hard sub-problems). Criticality emerges because the MILP's solve time is dominated by the hardest region: a single large, densely interconnected region can bottleneck the entire branch-and-bound process. Heterogeneity also challenges the EBM's learned representations, which must generalise across regions of very different sizes within the same scenario.

\paragraph{Remark on proxy nature.}
Several components are proxy-based because the generator does not necessarily store full time-series at metric-computation time (e.g., the categorical mapping in A4) and because some operational parameters are fixed in the current implementation (e.g., B5).
This appendix documents the \emph{exact} definitions used in the published code.

\section{Detailed MILP constraint formulation}
\label{app:milp_constraints}

This appendix provides the complete mathematical formulation of the nine constraint families summarized in Section~\ref{sec:milp_constraints}. All notation follows the definitions of Section~\ref{sec:milp_variables}.

\subsection{Thermal unit commitment}

Thermal output is bounded by the commitment binary and subject to symmetric inter-temporal ramp limits:
\begin{align}
\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z \, u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}
&\leq p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}
\leq \overline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z \, u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t},
& \forall\, z,t, \label{eq:thermal_bounds} \\
p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} - p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t-1}
&\leq R^{\mathrm{th}}_z,
& \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1, \label{eq:thermal_ramp_up} \\
p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t-1} - p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}
&\leq R^{\mathrm{th}}_z,
& \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1. \label{eq:thermal_ramp_down}
\end{align}
Here $\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z$ and $\overline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z$ denote the minimum and maximum stable generation levels, and $R^{\mathrm{th}}_z$ is the per-period ramp limit.
The startup indicator is linked to the commitment trajectory as:
\begin{equation}
v^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} \geq u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} - u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t-1},
\quad \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1.
\label{eq:thermal_startup}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Nuclear baseload}

Nuclear units are always-online baseload with limited modulation:
\begin{align}
\underline{P}^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z
&\leq p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t}
\leq \overline{P}^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z,
& \forall\, z,t, \label{eq:nuclear_bounds} \\
p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t} - p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t-1}
&\leq R^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z,
& \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1, \label{eq:nuclear_ramp_up} \\
p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t-1} - p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t}
&\leq R^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z,
& \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1, \label{eq:nuclear_ramp_down}
\end{align}
with $R^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z = 0.5\,\overline{P}^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z$.

\subsection{Renewable generation and curtailment}

Solar and wind output equal available power minus curtailment:
\begin{equation}
p^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t} + s^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t} = \overline{P}^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t}, \quad
p^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t} + s^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t} = \overline{P}^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t}, \quad \forall\, z,t.
\label{eq:renewable_balance}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Demand response}

Total shed equals the sum of tiered blocks, each bounded by its capacity:
\begin{equation}
d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}
= \textstyle\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} d^{\mathrm{dr},k}_{z,t}, \quad
d^{\mathrm{dr},k}_{z,t} \leq \overline{D}^{k}_{z,t}, \quad
d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t} \leq \overline{D}_{z,t},
\quad \forall\, z,t,k.
\label{eq:dr_blocks}
\end{equation}

The activation binary $\delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}$ links discrete activation decisions to
continuous shedding through big-M constraints:
\begin{equation}
\epsilon \, \delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}
\leq d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}
\leq \overline{D}_{z,t} \, \delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t},
\quad \forall\, z,t,
\label{eq:dr_activation}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon > 0$ enforces a minimum non-zero shed whenever demand response is activated.
The total number of activation events per zone is capped:
\begin{equation}
\textstyle\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \delta^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}
\leq N^{\mathrm{act}}_z,
\quad \forall\, z.
\label{eq:dr_max_activations}
\end{equation}

A rebound energy backlog tracks deferred demand and must be sufficiently cleared by the end
of the horizon:
\begin{equation}
e^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}
= (1 - \gamma)\, e^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t-1}
+ \Delta t \, d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}, \quad
e^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,T-1} \leq \bar{\varepsilon}^{\mathrm{dr}},
\quad \forall\, z,t,
\label{eq:dr_rebound}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ is the rebound decay rate and $\bar{\varepsilon}^{\mathrm{dr}}$ is the
end-of-horizon tolerance on deferred energy.

Inter-temporal ramping constraints smooth demand-response activation:
\begin{align}
d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t} - d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t-1}
&\leq R^{\mathrm{dr}}_z,
& \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1, \label{eq:dr_ramp_up} \\
d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t-1} - d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t}
&\leq R^{\mathrm{dr}}_z,
& \forall\, z,\, t \geq 1. \label{eq:dr_ramp_down}
\end{align}

\subsection{Battery storage}

Charge and discharge are bounded by rated power and mutually exclusive via the mode binary:
\begin{align}
b^{+}_{z,t} &\leq \overline{B}_z \, \sigma^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t}, &
b^{-}_{z,t} &\leq \overline{B}_z \, (1 - \sigma^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t}), & \forall\, z,t.
\label{eq:battery_mode}
\end{align}
The state of charge evolves with charging and discharging efficiencies ($\eta^{+}, \eta^{-}$) and self-discharge retention~$\rho^{\mathrm{bat}}_z$:
\begin{equation}
e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t} = \rho^{\mathrm{bat}}_z \, e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t-1} + \Delta t \bigl( \eta^{+} b^{+}_{z,t} - b^{-}_{z,t} / \eta^{-} \bigr), \quad
0 \leq e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t} \leq \overline{E}^{\mathrm{bat}}_z,
\label{eq:battery_soc}
\end{equation}
with final SOC bounded within a tolerance band around the initial level:
\begin{equation}
\underline{E}^{\mathrm{bat,fin}}_z \leq e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,T-1} \leq \overline{E}^{\mathrm{bat,fin}}_z.
\label{eq:battery_final}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reservoir hydro}

Hydro reservoirs receive natural inflows and may release power or spill excess water:
\begin{equation}
e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_{z,t} = e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_{z,t-1} + \Delta t \bigl( I^{\mathrm{hyd}}_{z,t} - h^{\mathrm{rel}}_{z,t} - h^{\mathrm{spl}}_{z,t} \bigr), \quad
h^{\mathrm{rel}}_{z,t} \leq \overline{H}_z, \quad
e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_{z,t} \leq \overline{E}^{\mathrm{hyd}}_z.
\label{eq:hydro_reservoir}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Pumped-hydro storage}

Pumped-hydro mirrors the battery formulation with its own mode binary, efficiencies, retention factor, and final level bounds:
\begin{align}
g^{+}_{z,t} &\leq \overline{G}_z \, \sigma^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t}, &
g^{-}_{z,t} &\leq \overline{G}_z \, (1 - \sigma^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t}), & \forall\, z,t, \label{eq:pumped_mode} \\
e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t} &= \rho^{\mathrm{phs}}_z \, e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t-1} + \Delta t \bigl( \eta^{+}_{\mathrm{p}} g^{+}_{z,t} - g^{-}_{z,t} / \eta^{-}_{\mathrm{p}} \bigr), &
0 \leq e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t} &\leq \overline{E}^{\mathrm{phs}}_z. \label{eq:pumped_soc}
\end{align}
Final level bounds $\underline{E}^{\mathrm{phs,fin}}_z \leq e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,T-1} \leq \overline{E}^{\mathrm{phs,fin}}_z$ are enforced analogously to battery storage.

\subsection{Transmission network and cross-border exchanges}

Power flows on each line are bounded by capacity (bidirectional):
\begin{equation}
-\overline{F}_\ell \leq f_{\ell,t} \leq \overline{F}_\ell, \quad \forall\, \ell \in \mathcal{L},\, t \in \mathcal{T}.
\label{eq:transmission}
\end{equation}
Import and export are mutually exclusive via a binary mode variable and bounded by the aggregate cross-border capacity $\overline{M}$:
\begin{equation}
m^{+}_{t} \leq \overline{M} \, \sigma^{\mathrm{imp}}_{t}, \quad
m^{-}_{t} \leq \overline{M} \, (1 - \sigma^{\mathrm{imp}}_{t}), \quad \forall\, t.
\label{eq:import_export}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Nodal power balance}

At every zone and time step, supply must equal demand:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\underbrace{p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} + p^{\mathrm{nuc}}_{z,t} + p^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t}
+ p^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t} + h^{\mathrm{rel}}_{z,t} + p^{\mathrm{ror}}_{z,t}
+ b^{-}_{z,t} + g^{-}_{z,t}}_{\text{generation}}
&+ d^{\mathrm{dr}}_{z,t} + q^{\mathrm{uns}}_{z,t}
+ \!\!\sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{to}}_z}\!\! f_{\ell,t}
+ \mathbb{1}_{[z=z_0]}(m^{+}_{t} - m^{-}_{t}) \\
&= D_{z,t} + b^{+}_{z,t} + g^{+}_{z,t}
+ \!\!\sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{from}}_z}\!\! f_{\ell,t}
+ q^{\mathrm{ovg}}_{z,t},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:power_balance}
\end{equation}
where $D_{z,t}$ is the exogenous demand, $p^{\mathrm{ror}}_{z,t}$ is the must-take run-of-river hydro, $\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{to}}_z$ and $\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{from}}_z$ are lines arriving at and departing from zone~$z$, and $z_0$ is the designated anchor zone for cross-border exchanges.

\section{Graph schema and encoder architecture details}
\label{app:graph_hte_details}

This appendix provides the formal definitions, complete edge-type catalogue, and architectural equations summarised in Section~\ref{sec:graph_encoder}.

\subsection{Graph definition}
\label{app:graph_schema}

Each scenario~$s$ is represented as a heterogeneous attributed graph
$\mathcal{G}(s) = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \phi_V, \phi_E, \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{A})$,
where $\mathcal{V}$ is the node set, $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{V} \times \mathcal{V}$ the edge set, $\phi_V : \mathcal{V} \to \mathcal{C}_V$ assigns each node to one of five types, $\phi_E : \mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{C}_E$ assigns each edge to one of eleven types, and $\mathbf{X}$, $\mathbf{A}$ collect typed node and edge feature vectors respectively.

\paragraph{Node types.}
\emph{Nation} ($|\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{nat}}|{=}1$, $\mathbb{R}^{10}$): system-wide aggregates.
\emph{Region} ($|\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{reg}}|{=}|\mathcal{R}|$, $\mathbb{R}^{10}$): regional capacities, weather metadata.
\emph{Zone} ($|\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{zon}}|{=}|\mathcal{Z}|$, $\mathbb{R}^{10}$): per-zone capacity, demand.
\emph{Asset} (variable count, $\mathbb{R}^{8}$): rated capacity, marginal cost, ramp rate, storage parameters, parent index; created only when capacity $>0$.
\emph{Weather} ($|\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{wth}}|{=}|\mathcal{R}|$, $\mathbb{R}^{5}$): spread intensity, profile encoding.
All vectors are zero-padded to $d_{\max}$ before batching.

\paragraph{Spatial edges (7 types).}
Containment: nation$\to$region, region$\to$zone, zone$\to$zonal asset, region$\to$regional asset.
Weather influence: weather$\to$zone, weather$\to$renewable asset.
Transmission: zone$\leftrightarrow$zone (bidirectional, attributes: capacity, distance).

\paragraph{Temporal extension.}
Each base node is replicated $T$ times; features are augmented with a sinusoidal positional encoding ($x^{\mathrm{supra}}_{(v,t)} = [x_v(t) \| \tau(t)]$).
Spatial edges are replicated at each time step. Four temporal edge families are added: SOC edges for storage (type~8), ramp edges for thermal/nuclear (type~9), DR cooldown edges (type~10), and temporal self-loops for stateful assets (type~7).

\subsection{Hierarchical Temporal Encoder: architecture and training details}
\label{app:hte_details}

\subsubsection*{Input projection}

Each supra-node feature vector $x^{\mathrm{supra}}_{(v,t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\max} + F_t}$ is projected to hidden dimension $D$:
\begin{equation}
h^{(0)}_{v,t} = W_{\mathrm{in}}\, x^{\mathrm{supra}}_{(v,t)} + b_{\mathrm{in}}, \qquad W_{\mathrm{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times (d_{\max}+F_t)}.
\end{equation}
The result is reshaped into $H^{(0)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{\mathrm{base}} \times T \times D}$.

\subsubsection*{Phase~1: Bottom-up spatial encoding}

At each level $\ell \in \{\text{asset}, \text{zone}, \text{region}\}$, $L_s$ GATv2 layers~\citep{brody_how_2022} ($H$ heads, $D/H$ dim per head, no concatenation) update representations via:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{h}^{(l)} = \mathrm{GATv2}\!\bigl(h^{(l-1)},\, \mathcal{E}_\ell\bigr), \quad
h^{(l)} = h^{(l-1)} + \mathrm{Dropout}\!\bigl(\mathrm{GELU}\!\bigl(\mathrm{LayerNorm}(\tilde{h}^{(l)})\bigr)\bigr).
\label{eq:gat_block}
\end{equation}
Edge sets $\mathcal{E}_\ell$: full supra-graph at asset level, intra-region FC at zone level, all-to-all at region level. After each block, a skip connection is saved and mean-pooling aggregates to the next coarser level:
\begin{equation}
h^{\mathrm{pool}}_{c,t} = \frac{1}{|\pi^{-1}(c)|} \sum_{v \in \pi^{-1}(c)} h^{(L_s)}_{v,t}.
\label{eq:pool}
\end{equation}
A final mean over regions yields $H_{\mathrm{nat}} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times T \times D}$.

\subsubsection*{Phase~2: Temporal Transformer}

A Transformer encoder~\citep{vaswani2017attention} ($L_t$ layers, $H$ heads, FFN width $4D$, sinusoidal positional encoding):
\begin{equation}
H^{\mathrm{temp}}_{\mathrm{nat}} = \mathrm{TransformerEncoder}\!\bigl(\mathrm{PosEnc}(H_{\mathrm{nat}})\bigr) \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times T \times D}.
\label{eq:temporal_transformer}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Phase~3: Top-down decoding}

Skip connections and GATv2 decoders propagate the nation representation back:
\begin{align}
H^{\mathrm{dec}}_{\mathrm{reg}} &= \mathrm{GATv2}_{\mathrm{reg}}^{\mathrm{dec}}\!\Bigl(\mathrm{broadcast}(H^{\mathrm{temp}}_{\mathrm{nat}}) + H^{\mathrm{skip}}_{\mathrm{reg}}\Bigr), \label{eq:topdown_reg} \\
H^{\mathrm{dec}}_{\mathrm{zon}} &= \mathrm{GATv2}_{\mathrm{zon}}^{\mathrm{dec}}\!\Bigl(\mathrm{unpool}_{\pi_{\mathrm{z \to r}}}(H^{\mathrm{dec}}_{\mathrm{reg}}) + H^{\mathrm{skip}}_{\mathrm{zon}}\Bigr), \label{eq:topdown_zon} \\
H^{\mathrm{dec}}_{\mathrm{ast}} &= \mathrm{GATv2}_{\mathrm{ast}}^{\mathrm{dec}}\!\Bigl(\mathrm{unpool}_{\pi_{\mathrm{a \to z}}}(H^{\mathrm{dec}}_{\mathrm{zon}}) + H^{\mathrm{skip}}_{\mathrm{ast}}\Bigr), \label{eq:topdown_ast}
\end{align}
where $h^{\mathrm{unpool}}_{v,t} = h^{\mathrm{dec}}_{\pi(v),t}$ indexes into the parent embedding.

\subsubsection*{Output}

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{HTE}(s) = \bigl\{
  H_{\mathrm{ast}} \!\in\! \mathbb{R}^{N_a \times T \times D},\;
  H_{\mathrm{zon}} \!\in\! \mathbb{R}^{N_z \times T \times D},\;
  H_{\mathrm{reg}} \!\in\! \mathbb{R}^{N_r \times T \times D},\;
  H_{\mathrm{nat}} \!\in\! \mathbb{R}^{T \times D}
\bigr\}.
\label{eq:hte_output}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Multi-lag InfoNCE loss}

Anchor--positive pairs at lags $\Lambda = \{1, 4, 8\}$ on $\ell_2$-normalised asset embeddings:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NCE}} = -\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|} \sum_{(a,p) \in \mathcal{P}} \log \frac{\exp(\mathrm{sim}(a, p) / \kappa)}{\exp(\mathrm{sim}(a, p) / \kappa) + \sum_{j=1}^{M_{\mathrm{neg}}} \exp(\mathrm{sim}(a, n_j) / \kappa)},
\label{eq:infonce}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{sim}(a, b) = a^\top b / (\|a\|\|b\|)$ and $\kappa$ is a temperature parameter.

\section{Energy-based model: architecture and training details}
\label{app:ebm_details}

This appendix provides the complete mathematical specification of the energy model, Langevin sampler, and loss functions summarised in Section~\ref{sec:ebm}.

\subsection{Energy function}

\paragraph{Input projection.}
At each zone~$z$ and time step~$t$, the binary decision vector $u_{z,t} \in \{0,1\}^F$ ($F{=}7$) is concatenated with the zone-level HTE embedding $h_{z,t} \in \mathbb{R}^D$ and projected:
\begin{equation}
x_{z,t} = \mathrm{SiLU}\!\bigl(\mathrm{LayerNorm}(W_{\mathrm{in}}\,[u_{z,t} \| h_{z,t}] + b_{\mathrm{in}})\bigr), \quad W_{\mathrm{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times (F+D)},
\label{eq:ebm_proj}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the hidden dimension and $\|$ denotes concatenation.

\paragraph{Temporal encoding.}
The projected sequence $\{x_{z,t}\}_{t=0}^{T-1}$ is processed by a bidirectional GRU with $L_g$ layers:
\begin{equation}
\{\tilde{x}_{z,t}\}_{t=0}^{T-1} = \mathrm{BiGRU}\!\bigl(\{x_{z,t}\}_{t=0}^{T-1}\bigr), \quad \tilde{x}_{z,t} \in \mathbb{R}^{2H}.
\label{eq:ebm_gru}
\end{equation}
The bidirectional architecture allows each hidden state to incorporate both past and future context within the zone's trajectory---critical for detecting temporal patterns such as start-up/shut-down sequences and charge/discharge cycles.

\paragraph{Energy head.}
A three-layer MLP maps each hidden state to a scalar per-zone, per-timestep energy contribution:
\begin{equation}
e_{z,t} = W_3 \,\mathrm{SiLU}(W_2\,\mathrm{SiLU}(\mathrm{Dropout}(\mathrm{LayerNorm}(W_1 \tilde{x}_{z,t} + b_1))) + b_2) + b_3, \quad e_{z,t} \in \mathbb{R}.
\label{eq:ebm_head}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Mean energy.}
The mean energy aggregates over valid zones and timesteps:
\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{mean}} = \frac{\sum_{z,t} m_z \, e_{z,t}}{\sum_{z} m_z \cdot T},
\label{eq:ebm_mean}
\end{equation}
where $m_z \in \{0,1\}$ is the zone mask.

\paragraph{Peak energy via masked log-sum-exp.}
For each zone, a soft-max over timesteps extracts the peak energy:
\begin{equation}
\hat{e}_z = \tau \log \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \exp\!\bigl(e_{z,t} / \tau\bigr), \quad
E_{\mathrm{peak}} = \frac{\sum_{z} m_z \, \hat{e}_z}{\sum_{z} m_z},
\label{eq:ebm_peak}
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is a sharpness parameter. As $\tau \to 0$, $\hat{e}_z \to \max_t e_{z,t}$; larger~$\tau$ smooths the peak towards the mean. In our implementation, padded timesteps of masked zones are set to $-\infty$ before the log-sum-exp.

\paragraph{Total energy.}
\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{raw}} = (1 - w_p)\,E_{\mathrm{mean}} + w_p\,E_{\mathrm{peak}}, \quad
E_\theta(u, h) = E_{\max} \cdot \tanh\!\bigl(E_{\mathrm{raw}} / E_{\max}\bigr),
\label{eq:ebm_total}
\end{equation}
where $w_p$ is the peak weight and $E_{\max}$ bounds the energy to $[-E_{\max}, E_{\max}]$.

\paragraph{Weight initialisation.}
All linear layers use Xavier normal initialisation with gain~$1.0$; biases are initialised to zero. This choice, combined with the energy bounding, ensures that the initial energy landscape is nearly flat, allowing the contrastive divergence training to shape it gradually.

\subsection{Langevin sampler}

\paragraph{Logit-space dynamics.}
Decisions are parameterised as $u = \sigma(z)$ with $z \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times Z \times T \times F}$. The update rule at step~$k$ is:
\begin{equation}
z^{(k+1)} = z^{(k)} - \eta \, \hat{g}^{(k)} - \eta \, \lambda_p \bigl(z^{(k)} - z_{\mathrm{prior}}\bigr) + \sigma_k \sqrt{\eta} \, \varepsilon^{(k)},
\label{eq:langevin_full}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{g}^{(k)} = \nabla_z E / \mathrm{std}(\nabla_z E)$ is the normalised gradient, $\lambda_p$ is a prior-drift strength (optionally biasing towards a sparse prior $z_{\mathrm{prior}} = \mathrm{logit}(p_0)$), and $\varepsilon^{(k)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

\paragraph{Temperature schedule.}
The noise scale follows a linear anneal:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_k = \sigma_{\max} + \frac{k}{K-1}\,(\sigma_{\min} - \sigma_{\max}),
\label{eq:temp_schedule}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the total number of steps. High initial temperature encourages broad exploration; low final temperature sharpens the samples towards local energy minima.

\paragraph{Initialisation.}
Four modes are available:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \texttt{soft}: $z^{(0)} \sim \mathcal{N}(z_{\mathrm{prior}}, 0.01)$ (default, near-sparse start).
  \item \texttt{prior}: $z^{(0)} = z_{\mathrm{prior}}$ (deterministic sparse start).
  \item \texttt{bernoulli}: $z^{(0)} = \mathrm{logit}(\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p_0))$ (random binary start).
  \item \texttt{oracle}: $z^{(0)} = \mathrm{logit}(u^*)$ (warm-start from MILP solution, used for diagnostics).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Binarisation (inference).}
The final relaxed output $u_{\mathrm{relax}} = \sigma(z^{(K)})$ is converted to binary via either:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Threshold: $\hat{u}_{z,t,f} = \mathbb{1}[u_{\mathrm{relax},z,t,f} > 0.5]$.
  \item Bernoulli: $\hat{u}_{z,t,f} \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(u_{\mathrm{relax},z,t,f})$ (stochastic, more diverse).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Loss functions}

\paragraph{Contrastive divergence.}
The CD loss is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CD}} = \bar{E}_{\mathrm{pos}} - \bar{E}_{\mathrm{neg}} + \alpha_{\mathrm{reg}} \Bigl(
  \bigl[\lvert \bar{E}_{\mathrm{pos}} \rvert - 30\bigr]_+^2 + \bigl[\lvert \bar{E}_{\mathrm{neg}} \rvert - 30\bigr]_+^2
\Bigr),
\label{eq:cd_full}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{E}$ denotes batch means, $[\cdot]_+ = \max(\cdot, 0)$, and the regularisation term only activates when energy magnitudes exceed~30, preventing collapse without interfering with normal training dynamics.

\paragraph{Preference loss.}
For candidate pairs ranked by LP cost ($c_a < c_b$):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pref}} = \bigl[\,E_\theta(u_a, h) - E_\theta(u_b, h) + \delta\,\bigr]_+,
\label{eq:pref_full}
\end{equation}
where $\delta$ is a margin hyperparameter.

\paragraph{Combined silver loss.}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{silver}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{CD}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CD}} + \lambda_{\mathrm{pref}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pref}}.
\label{eq:combined_full}
\end{equation}

\section{Feasibility decoder and LP worker details}
\label{app:reconstruction_details}

This appendix provides the complete algorithmic specification of the feasibility decoder and the two-stage LP worker summarised in Section~\ref{sec:reconstruction}.

\subsection{Scenario physics}

The decoder operates on a \texttt{ScenarioPhysics} dataclass that encapsulates all physical and operational parameters needed to produce a feasible dispatch. The key fields are:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Topology:} number of zones~$Z$, timesteps~$T$, regions, zone-to-region mapping, anchor zone index.
  \item \textbf{Time series} $[Z \times T]$: demand $D_{z,t}$, solar available $\overline{P}^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t}$, wind available $\overline{P}^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t}$, run-of-river hydro $p^{\mathrm{ror}}_{z,t}$, hydro inflow $I^{\mathrm{hyd}}_{z,t}$.
  \item \textbf{Per-zone scalars} $[Z]$: battery power $\overline{B}_z$ and capacity $\overline{E}^{\mathrm{bat}}_z$, pumped-hydro power $\overline{G}_z$ and capacity $\overline{E}^{\mathrm{phs}}_z$, thermal capacity $\overline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z$ and minimum $\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z$, nuclear capacity $\overline{P}^{\mathrm{nuc}}_z$, hydro reservoir capacity (MW and MWh), DR capacity.
  \item \textbf{Storage efficiencies:} separate charge/discharge efficiencies $\eta^+_{\mathrm{bat}}, \eta^-_{\mathrm{bat}}, \eta^+_{\mathrm{phs}}, \eta^-_{\mathrm{phs}}$ and self-discharge retention rates $\rho^{\mathrm{bat}}_z, \rho^{\mathrm{phs}}_z$.
  \item \textbf{Thermal dynamics:} ramp limit $R^{\mathrm{th}}_z$, initial output $p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,0}$, startup cost, marginal cost.
  \item \textbf{Initial states:} battery SOC, pumped-hydro level, hydro reservoir level (all as fractions of capacity).
\end{itemize}

These fields are populated from the scenario JSON via the MILP scenario loader, ensuring exact consistency with the data used by the MILP oracle.

\subsection{Feasibility decoder algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:decoder} provides the complete pseudocode. The decoder returns a \texttt{FeasiblePlan} containing both binary decisions and continuous dispatch values for all resource types.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{EBM-Guided Feasibility Decoder (v5)}
\label{alg:decoder}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Binary candidate $\hat{u} \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times 7}$, scenario physics $\mathcal{P}$
\Ensure Feasible plan $(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{e})$ with binary decisions, continuous dispatch, and storage states

\State Initialise SOC: $e^{\mathrm{bat}}_z \gets \mathrm{SOC}_0^{\mathrm{bat}} \cdot \overline{E}^{\mathrm{bat}}_z$, \; $e^{\mathrm{phs}}_z \gets \mathrm{SOC}_0^{\mathrm{phs}} \cdot \overline{E}^{\mathrm{phs}}_z$, \; $e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_z \gets E_0^{\mathrm{hyd}}$
\State Initialise $p^{\mathrm{th,prev}}_z \gets p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,\mathrm{init}}$, \; $h^{\mathrm{dr}}_z \gets 0$ \Comment{Thermal history, DR hours used}

\For{$t = 0, \dots, T{-}1$}
  \State Update hydro: $e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_z \gets \min(e^{\mathrm{hyd}}_z + I_{z,t} \Delta t, \, \overline{E}^{\mathrm{hyd}}_z)$
  \State Apply self-discharge: $e^{\mathrm{bat}}_z \gets \rho^{\mathrm{bat}}_z \cdot e^{\mathrm{bat}}_z$, \; $e^{\mathrm{phs}}_z \gets \rho^{\mathrm{phs}}_z \cdot e^{\mathrm{phs}}_z$
  \State $r_z \gets D_{z,t} - p^{\mathrm{nuc,must}}_z - \overline{P}^{\mathrm{sol}}_{z,t} - \overline{P}^{\mathrm{wnd}}_{z,t} - p^{\mathrm{ror}}_{z,t}$ \Comment{Residual demand}

  \For{each zone $z$ with $r_z > 0$} \Comment{Phase 2: Deficit}
    \State Dispatch nuclear headroom, hydro reservoir
    \State Dispatch EBM-suggested storage discharge (pumped, battery) if $\hat{u}$ flags active
    \State Dispatch EBM-suggested thermal (ramp-aware) if $\hat{u}$ flags on
    \State \textit{Fallback:} dispatch remaining storage, thermal, imports, DR, unserved
  \EndFor

  \For{each zone $z$ with $r_z < 0$} \Comment{Phase 3: Surplus}
    \State Export (anchor zone only), reduce thermal (ramp-aware)
    \State Curtail wind, then solar
    \State Charge EBM-suggested storage (pumped, battery), then fallback charge
    \State Spill remaining overgeneration
  \EndFor

  \State $p^{\mathrm{th,prev}}_z \gets p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t}$ \Comment{Update thermal history}
  \State $e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t+1} \gets e^{\mathrm{bat}}_z$, \; $e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t+1} \gets e^{\mathrm{phs}}_z$
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Thermal ramp enforcement.}
When dispatching thermal generation at time~$t$, the maximum output is clamped to:
\begin{equation}
p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} \leq \min\!\bigl(\overline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z,\; p^{\mathrm{th,prev}}_z + R^{\mathrm{th}}_z\bigr).
\label{eq:decoder_ramp}
\end{equation}
A startup exception applies: if $p^{\mathrm{th,prev}}_z = 0$ (unit was off), the decoder allows $p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = \underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z$ even if this exceeds the ramp limit, matching the MILP's implicit startup behaviour. Similarly, ramp-down is enforced during surplus handling:
\begin{equation}
p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} \geq \max\!\bigl(\underline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z,\; p^{\mathrm{th,prev}}_z - R^{\mathrm{th}}_z\bigr) \quad \text{if } u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = 1.
\label{eq:decoder_ramp_down}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Storage SOC tracking.}
At each time step, the SOC is updated using the MILP-consistent dynamics:
\begin{align}
e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t+1} &= \rho^{\mathrm{bat}}_z \, e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t} + \Delta t \bigl(\eta^+_{\mathrm{bat}} \, b^+_{z,t} - b^-_{z,t} / \eta^-_{\mathrm{bat}}\bigr), \label{eq:decoder_soc_bat} \\
e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t+1} &= \rho^{\mathrm{phs}}_z \, e^{\mathrm{phs}}_{z,t} + \Delta t \bigl(\eta^+_{\mathrm{phs}} \, g^+_{z,t} - g^-_{z,t} / \eta^-_{\mathrm{phs}}\bigr). \label{eq:decoder_soc_phs}
\end{align}
Charge and discharge are bounded by both the rated power and the available headroom/energy:
\begin{equation}
b^+_{z,t} \leq \min\!\Bigl(\overline{B}_z, \; \frac{\overline{E}^{\mathrm{bat}}_z - e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t}}{\eta^+_{\mathrm{bat}} \, \Delta t}\Bigr), \qquad
b^-_{z,t} \leq \min\!\Bigl(\overline{B}_z, \; \frac{e^{\mathrm{bat}}_{z,t} \, \eta^-_{\mathrm{bat}}}{\Delta t}\Bigr).
\label{eq:decoder_storage_bounds}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Demand-response accounting.}
A running counter $h^{\mathrm{dr}}_z$ tracks the cumulative DR activation duration per zone. DR is only dispatched if $h^{\mathrm{dr}}_z < h^{\mathrm{dr}}_{\max}$ (default: 4 hours), after which the zone's DR capacity is exhausted for the remainder of the horizon.


\subsection{LP worker: deviation penalty formulation}

The LP worker builds on the full MILP model (Section~\ref{sec:milp_objective}--\ref{sec:milp_constraints}) with the following modifications at repair stages.

\paragraph{Deviation variables.}
For each (zone, time) pair $(z, t)$ in the unfixed subset $\mathcal{S}$, auxiliary non-negative variables $d^+_{z,t}, d^-_{z,t} \geq 0$ are introduced for each binary family $u \in \{u^{\mathrm{th}}, \sigma^{\mathrm{bat}}, \sigma^{\mathrm{phs}}, \delta^{\mathrm{dr}}\}$:
\begin{equation}
u_{z,t} - \hat{u}_{z,t} = d^+_{z,t} - d^-_{z,t}, \quad \forall\, (z,t) \in \mathcal{S},
\label{eq:lp_deviation}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{u}_{z,t}$ is the decoder's target value.

\paragraph{Penalised objective.}
The original UC objective $f(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{u})$ is augmented:
\begin{equation}
\min \; f(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{u}) + \lambda \!\!\sum_{(z,t) \in \mathcal{S}} \bigl(d^+_{z,t} + d^-_{z,t}\bigr),
\label{eq:lp_penalised}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda = 10\,000$ for thermal commitment (the dominant binary family) and $\lambda/2$ for storage modes and DR activation. The high penalty ensures that deviations occur only when necessary for feasibility.

\paragraph{Flip budget constraint.}
\begin{equation}
\sum_{(z,t) \in \mathcal{S}} \bigl(d^+_{z,t} + d^-_{z,t}\bigr) \leq B,
\label{eq:lp_flip_budget}
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the stage-dependent budget (10\% or 50\% of total binaries). Since each binary is $\{0,1\}$ and each target is $\{0,1\}$, a single flip costs exactly~1, so $B$ directly limits the number of changed decisions.

\paragraph{True objective recovery.}
After solving, the LP worker reports the \emph{original} UC objective $f(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{u})$ (without the penalty term) as the solution cost. The penalty term is logged separately for diagnostics but is not included in the reported objective, ensuring fair comparison with the MILP oracle.


\subsection{Critical index selection}

The heuristic for selecting which (zone, time) pairs to unfix in Stages~2--3 uses a composite score:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{score}(z, t) = 2\bigl(q^{\mathrm{uns}}_{z,t} + q^{\mathrm{ovg}}_{z,t}\bigr) + \mathrm{tension}(z, t),
\label{eq:critical_score}
\end{equation}
where $q^{\mathrm{uns}}_{z,t}$ and $q^{\mathrm{ovg}}_{z,t}$ are the unserved energy and overgeneration from the previous stage's solution, and the tension term captures thermal capacity mismatch:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{tension}(z, t) = \begin{cases}
0.5\,\overline{P}^{\mathrm{th}}_z & \text{if } q^{\mathrm{uns}}_{z,t} > 0 \text{ and } u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = 0 \text{ but } \hat{u}^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = 1, \\
0.5\,p^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} & \text{if } q^{\mathrm{ovg}}_{z,t} > 0 \text{ and } u^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = 1 \text{ but } \hat{u}^{\mathrm{th}}_{z,t} = 0, \\
0 & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:tension}
\end{equation}
The top-$K$ pairs are selected, and each is expanded by a temporal window ($\pm 1$ time step) to capture inter-temporal coupling effects (ramp constraints, SOC continuity).

\section{Ablation study: pipeline without decoder}
\label{app:no_decoder}

This appendix presents an ablation study that removes the Hierarchical Feasibility Decoder (Section~\ref{sec:reconstruction}) from the pipeline, passing the EBM's raw binary samples directly to the LP Worker. The goal is to isolate the decoder's contribution to solution quality and solve speed.

\subsection{Architectural difference}

In the baseline pipeline, the decoder receives the EBM's binary candidate $\hat{u} \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times 7}$ and produces a modified binary decision vector along with a continuous warm-start dispatch plan (Algorithm~\ref{alg:decoder}). The LP Worker then solves the unit commitment LP with the decoder's binaries fixed and the continuous variables warm-started from the decoder's dispatch.

In the no-decoder ablation, the EBM's raw binary samples are passed directly to the LP Worker without modification. Two consequences follow:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{No binary correction:} the greedy heuristic that adjusts EBM decisions based on merit-order dispatch and physical constraints is bypassed. The LP Worker receives the EBM's unfiltered predictions.
  \item \textbf{No LP warm-start:} since the decoder also produces the continuous dispatch values used to initialise the LP solver's basis, removing it eliminates the warm-start capability. The LP solver starts from scratch at each repair stage.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results on extreme-criticality scenarios}

Table~\ref{tab:no_decoder_comparison} compares the no-decoder ablation against the baseline (with decoder) on the 100 extreme-criticality scenarios of Section~\ref{sec:ood_extreme}.

\subsection{Key findings}

\paragraph{The decoder degrades EBM binary quality.}
Removing the decoder improves the median cost gap from 119.6\% to $-$0.4\%---a 120 percentage-point improvement. The no-decoder pipeline produces a lower objective than the baseline in 79 out of 100 scenarios and beats the MILP's own incumbent in 76 scenarios (cost gap $< 0$\%). This demonstrates that the decoder's greedy binary modifications---intended to enforce physical feasibility---are actively harmful to solution quality on these extreme instances. The EBM's raw predictions, when given sufficient LP repair budget, yield better solutions.

\paragraph{The decoder provides essential warm-start speed.}
The speed trade-off is equally stark: the no-decoder pipeline is $2.6\times$ slower (median 590.9\,s vs.\ 223.4\,s for the full best-of-5 pipeline). The stage distribution shifts dramatically from 48\% hard-fix to 79\% full-soft, because without the decoder's binary corrections the LP Worker must relax and re-optimise most binary variables from scratch. The loss of LP warm-starting accounts for most of the additional solve time: cumulative LP time across the 5 candidates rises from 214.7\,s to 586.7\,s on average ($\sim$117\,s per individual LP vs.\ $\sim$43\,s with the decoder's warm-start).

\paragraph{Stage transition analysis.}
The cross-tabulation of LP stages reveals that 73 out of 94 scenarios that were at hard-fix or repair-20 with the decoder transition to full-soft without it. These transitioning scenarios show a mean cost gap improvement of $-$320 percentage points, confirming that the full-soft stage---while slower---explores the solution space more effectively when starting from unmodified EBM binaries.

\paragraph{Economic advantage.}
The no-decoder pipeline achieves economic profitability (positive advantage over MILP) in 76\% of scenarios even at $\lambda = 10$\,EUR/s, compared to only 6\% for the baseline. Its breakeven $\lambda$ for 50\% profitability is effectively 0\,EUR/s (since most scenarios already have negative cost gaps), whereas the baseline requires $\lambda \approx 1\,000$\,EUR/s.

\subsection{Implications}

This ablation identifies the decoder as both the pipeline's \emph{quality bottleneck} and its \emph{speed enabler}. The ideal solution is not to remove the decoder entirely but to replace its greedy heuristic with a learned component that preserves the EBM's binary decisions while still generating a high-quality continuous warm-start for the LP solver (research avenue \textbf{R2} in Section~\ref{sec:research_avenues}).

\section{Ablation study: GNN dispatch predictor}
\label{app:gnn_dispatch}

This appendix presents an ablation study that replaces both the Feasibility Decoder and the LP Worker with a Graph Neural Network (GNN) that directly predicts continuous dispatch values from the EBM's binary decisions and HTE embeddings. The goal is to assess the speed--quality frontier when LP solving is entirely eliminated.

\subsection{Architectural difference}

The GNN Dispatch predictor (3.8\,M parameters) takes as input the EBM's binary candidate $\hat{u} \in \{0,1\}^{Z \times T \times 7}$ concatenated with zone-level HTE embeddings $h \in \mathbb{R}^{Z \times T \times D}$ and predicts continuous dispatch values for 11 channels:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = \mathrm{DispatchGNN}(\hat{u}, h) \in \mathbb{R}^{Z \times T \times 11},
\label{eq:gnn_dispatch}
\end{equation}
where the 11 output channels correspond to thermal, nuclear, solar, wind, battery charge/discharge, pumped-hydro charge/discharge, demand response, unserved energy, and hydro release. The model is trained on MILP ground-truth dispatch labels using an $\ell_1$ loss.

The pipeline comparison is:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Baseline:} HTE $\to$ EBM $\to$ Decoder $\to$ LP Worker (Decoder + LP $\approx$ 222\,s).
  \item \textbf{GNN Dispatch:} HTE $\to$ EBM $\to$ DispatchGNN (GNN $\approx$ 0.15\,s).
\end{itemize}
The GNN replaces the two most expensive pipeline stages with a single GPU-accelerated forward pass. However, it does not enforce any physical constraints (power balance, storage SOC continuity, ramp limits), so its output is not guaranteed to be feasible.

\subsection{Cost gap assessment}

Computing the operating cost from GNN-predicted dispatch (thermal fuel, nuclear fuel, storage cycling, DR, unserved energy, and renewable curtailment costs) yields a median cost gap of 183.8\% and a mean of 522.7\% relative to the MILP objective. These figures are higher than both the baseline (119.6\% median) and the no-decoder ablation ($-$0.4\% median), because the GNN's dispatch predictions do not account for startup costs, transmission losses, or import/export flows---components that contribute to the MILP objective but are absent from the GNN's output space.

\subsection{Implications}

The GNN Dispatch ablation demonstrates a clear \emph{speed--quality Pareto point}: at $82\times$ the baseline speed, it sacrifices solution optimality but provides dispatch predictions with sub-15\,MW accuracy per channel. This positions the GNN variant as a \emph{screening tool} for rapid scenario triage---identifying which scenarios merit full LP solving---rather than a replacement for the LP-based pipeline. A hybrid architecture that uses the GNN for initial dispatch estimation and triggers LP solving only when the predicted dispatch violates feasibility thresholds could combine the speed of the GNN with the quality guarantees of the LP worker.
